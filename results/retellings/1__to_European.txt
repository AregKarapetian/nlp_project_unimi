In days of yore, in a kingdom far and wide, there lived a king with three stalwart sons: Pedro, Diego, and Juan. The monarch, wise and just, decreed that his three sons should set forth into the world to seek their fortunes and make their names known. And so, the brothers took different paths, but before they parted ways, they agreed to meet at a certain spot in the forest, where the trees grew tall and the wildflowers bloomed.

After many days of travel, Don Juan chanced upon an old man on the road. The venerable sage offered Don Juan a loaf of bread and bade him proceed to a palace that lay but a mile distant. "As you enter the gate," said the old man with a twinkle in his eye, "divide the bread among the monkeys who guard the entrance. Otherwise, thou shalt not be granted entry."

Don Juan took the bread and followed the old man's instructions. As he entered the palace gates, a mighty monkey barred his path. The creature's countenance was fearsome, but Don Juan steeled himself and prepared to flee when the monkey spoke in a voice that rumbled like thunder: "Hail, Don Juan! I know thy purpose in coming here is to seek thy fortune. And behold, my daughter Chonguita shall be thine wife!"

The archbishop of the monkeys was summoned, and Don Juan and Chonguita were wedded forthwith. A few days passed, and Don Juan requested permission from his new bride to depart for the forest meeting place where he had agreed to meet his brothers.

Chonguita's mother, a wise and loving creature, overheard Don Juan's words and said to him: "If thou art departing, take Chonguita with thee." Though Don Juan was loath to do so, he was forced to acquiesce. And thus, the couple set forth together.

When they arrived at the appointed meeting place, Don Juan's brothers and their wives were astonished by his presence. Don Diego asked: "What ails thee, brother? Where is thy wife?" Don Juan replied with a heavy heart: "Behold, she stands behind me." His brothers' eyes widened in surprise as they beheld Chonguita, the monkey.

"What sorcery hath befallen thee, Don Juan?" exclaimed Don Pedro. "Didst thou lose thy wits?" Don Juan could not respond to their jests, and so he said: "Let us depart! Our father must await our return."

And with that, Don Juan turned about and began the journey homeward, Chonguita by his side. His brothers and their wives followed closely behind.

When the news of the three brothers' return reached the king's ears, he hastened to meet them on the palace stairs. But upon learning that one of his sons had married a monkey, the monarch fainted from shock. When he regained consciousness, he said to himself: "This misfortune is God's will. I must bear it with patience."

The king assigned separate dwellings for each couple, but his heart remained heavy with sorrow. He called his three sons together and decreed that their wives should create embroidered coats for him within three days' time. The penalty for failure would be death.

Don Juan's father hoped against hope that Chonguita would fail in this task, but she proved to be a skilled needlewoman. Her coat was the most exquisite of all, and the king's heart swelled with dismay.

Undeterred, the monarch next commanded his daughters-in-law to create caps for him within two days' time, under penalty of death if they failed. Chonguita once more surpassed her sisters-in-law in skill and beauty.

Finally, the king devised a plan to rid himself of the monkey wife. He summoned his three daughters-in-law and declared: "The husband of the one who shall create the most wondrous picture on the walls of my chamber within three days' time shall inherit the throne."

When the deadline arrived, the king inspected the artwork created by each wife. And lo! Chonguita's masterpiece was the most resplendent of all.

Don Juan was crowned king in a grand ceremony, and a great feast was held in his honor. But as the festivities reached their peak, Don Juan grew enraged with Chonguita for insisting that he dance with her. He hurled her against the wall, and the hall plunged into darkness.

Yet, after a brief interval, the lights returned, and Chonguita stood transformed before him – no longer a monkey, but a beautiful maiden.
