In a far-off kingdom, there lived a mighty Sultan who had a daughter as lovely as a lotus flower. The Sultan entrusted his daughter to a wise and venerable priest, instructing him to teach her the sacred teachings of the Koran.

The priest, however, harbored impure intentions towards the young girl. He coveted her like a miser covets gold, but she refused his advances with a firmness that would put a mountain to shame. One day, she said to him, "Come to me tomorrow," and when he arrived, she cleverly removed the ladder by which he used to ascend to her chambers.

The priest, enraged at being foiled, sent a deceitful letter to the Sultan, accusing his daughter of being a wanton woman. The Sultan, believing the lies, returned from his pilgrimage in a fury, intending to have his daughter's throat cut by some slaves.

But fate had other plans for the young girl. While the slaves dug her grave, she crawled away, using her cunning and agility to evade their blades. She hid among the trees, waiting for an opportunity to escape.

When the slaves returned with the Sultan, they poured a bottle of gazelle's blood into his presence, claiming it was the girl's lifeless body. But little did they know that the girl had been watching from afar, her heart filled with determination and resilience.

Days passed, and a caravan happened upon the wooded place where the girl lay hidden. As the travelers loaded their camels, one of them spotted the young girl sitting under a tree. A kind-hearted man took pity on her and brought her to his home in a nearby town.

Later, the Sultan's son chanced upon the girl's face and was struck by its beauty. He requested permission from the man who had taken her in to marry her, despite knowing that she had no legs. The man agreed, and the young couple was married.

As they lived together, the girl bore two children, and while pregnant, she had a prophetic dream. In it, two birds perched on her legs, which miraculously grew out. When morning broke, the girl indeed found her legs restored to their former strength.

With her newfound freedom, she took her children, a ram, and the two birds, and set out on the pilgrimage that her husband had promised to undertake. At a midway point, they encountered a magnificent building, where the Sultan, his daughter's father, and the priest were waiting in disguise.

The girl told tales of her ordeal to her children, and the Sultan listened intently, his heart filled with regret for having believed the priest's lies. The wise and just ruler then ordered the priest's throat to be cut, a fitting punishment for his treachery.

With the priest dealt with, the Sultan, his daughter, and her husband continued their pilgrimage together, finally reconciling after years of separation. And so, the girl found peace and happiness, her spirit unbroken like a lotus flower that blooms in the muddiest waters.
