In a bamboo forest, a cunning rabbit named Chonguita was strolling along, singing to himself as he picked wild berries for dinner. Suddenly, a shadow flitted across the path ahead, and Chonguita looked up to see a majestic hawk soaring overhead. The hawk, Mr. Kaito, circled lower and lower, his piercing gaze fixed on the rabbit below.

Chonguita, however, remained oblivious to the danger lurking above him. Down swooped Mr. Kaito, talons extended, and Chonguita felt himself lifted from the ground. The hawk's grip was like a vice, making it hard for Chonguita to breathe. He squirmed and struggled, but it was no use.

Finally, Chonguita lay still, thinking quickly about how to escape his predicament. "I'm not worth eating, Mr. Kaito," he said with a humble tone. "You're too kind to waste your time on me." Mr. Kaito scoffed at the idea, saying, "No, no! I'll make short work of you and then go catch those pesky jay-birds that have been stealing food from my nest."

Chonguita's eyes widened in alarm at the mention of jay-birds, his greatest nemesis. He begged Mr. Kaito to catch them first, saying, "Please, sir, do take care of those birds! They're a nuisance, and I'd rather stay here until you return." But Mr. Kaito saw through Chonguita's ruse, saying, "Ah, Brer Rabbit, you've fooled many before, but not me!"

Undeterred, Chonguita proposed another plan. "If you can't catch the jay-birds now, perhaps it would be better to wait until I'm tamer and less wild." Mr. Kaito's expression changed, and he seemed to consider this idea.

Chonguita pressed his advantage, saying, "Or maybe it would be best if you waited for me to grow bigger and more plump?" Mr. Kaito's eyes lit up at the prospect of a full meal. Chonguita continued, "And I'll even help you by driving out some partridges from their hiding places, so we can have a grand hunt together!"

Mr. Kaito was taken in by Chonguita's words and released him from his talons. But as soon as he was free, Chonguita made a run for it, leaving Mr. Kaito to wait in vain for the partridges and the plump rabbit that never came. The cunning Chonguita had outwitted the hawk once again, and escaped with his life.
