In days of yore, when the sun beat down upon the fields, a farmer's wife toiled away at her winnowing basket, separating grain from chaff with diligent hands. As she worked, a cunning crow swooped in, snatching a single grain and perching on a nearby tree to devour it. The farmer's wife, enraged by this thievery, hurled a clod of earth with such precision that the crow tumbled to the ground, dropping its prize into a narrow crack in the trunk.

The wretched bird, fearing for its life, promised to return the grain, but alas! when it came to search for it, the grain had rolled so far into the crevice that neither beak nor claw could retrieve it. Desperate and defeated, the crow flew off to seek aid from a woodcutter, who refused to lend his axe to the bird's cause.

Undeterred, the crow winged its way to the palace of the king, beseeching him to dispatch the man who had thwarted the crow's plans. But the monarch, unmoved by the bird's pleas, sent it packing with a stern refusal. The crow then flew on to the queen's chambers, begging her to coax the king into compliance, but she too refused.

As the crow continued its journey, it met a serpent who declined to bite the queen, and a stick that refused to strike the snake. Next, it encountered a fire that refused to consume the stick, followed by water that refused to quench the flames. The crow's quest led it to an ox, which politely declined to drink from the water, then to a rope that wouldn't bind the ox.

Finally, as the sun began to set, the crow chanced upon a mouse, who, with a haughty sniff, refused to gnaw through the rope. But when the crow mentioned the name of its nemesis, a sleek and cunning cat emerged from the shadows, her ears perked up and eyes blazing with hunger for the unsuspecting mouse.
