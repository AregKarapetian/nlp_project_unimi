In days of yore, when the sun beat down upon the fields, a farmer's wife toiled away, winnowing corn with diligent hands. As she worked, a cunning crow swooped down from the sky, snatching a grain from the basket and perching on a nearby tree to devour it. The good woman's ire was roused at this audacity, and with a swift motion, she hurled a clod of earth that struck the bird with deadly aim.

The crow tumbled to the ground, dropping its prize as it fell. The grain rolled into a narrow crevice in the tree trunk, where it lodged out of reach. The farmer's wife, her temper still aflame, rushed up to the fallen bird and seized it by the tail, demanding that it return her stolen grain lest she take its life.

The wretched crow, cowed by fear of death, promised to retrieve the grain, but alas! when it came to search for it, the kernel had rolled too far into the crack, beyond its beak or claw. In desperation, the bird flew off to seek aid from a woodsman, who dwelled in a nearby glade.

The woodman refused to lend his axe to cut the tree, and so the crow winged its way to the king's palace, hoping to persuade him to slay the man. But His Majesty would not be swayed, and the bird was forced to continue its quest.

It next sought out the queen, who in turn referred the matter to a snake that dwelled in the castle walls. The serpent refused to bite the queen, and thus the crow flew on, seeking aid from a stick that leaned against the wall.

The stick, however, would not strike the snake, and so the bird continued its journey, meeting fire, water, an ox, a rope, and finally, a mouse. Each of these, in turn, refused to carry out the task set before them, until at last the crow encountered a cat, who, upon hearing the word "mouse", gave chase with unyielding ferocity.

The world may end before a cat will leave a mouse alone, and so it was that the crow's quest came to an end.
