In the sweltering heat of summer's eve, Sennuccio and his wife Bedovina sat idly by the entrance of their humble abode, reluctant to retire for the night. "Bedovina," Sennuccio said with a sigh, "close the door, it is time for us to rest." But she replied with a lazy smile, "You may shut it yourself, my dear husband." The two continued to bicker, neither willing to concede, until Sennuccio proposed a wager: whoever spoke first would be responsible for shutting the door. Bedovina, obstinate as ever, agreed to this plan.

As they sat there, neither one dared to speak, fearing the penalty of having to close the door. But soon, Bedovina's eyelids grew heavy with sleep, and she rose from her seat, shedding her garments as she made her way to their bedroom. A short while later, a young servant boy passed by, carrying a lantern with a flickering candle. When the flame went out, he entered Sennuccio's house, calling out for someone to provide him with a light.

However, his cries fell on deaf ears, and he ventured further into the house. There, he spotted Sennuccio sitting motionless on a bench, his eyes wide open but refusing to speak. The servant boy took Sennuccio by the hand, shaking him gently, saying, "Good brother, what's amiss? Answer me quickly!" But Sennuccio remained silent, too afraid of incurring the penalty.

The servant boy then noticed a faint glow emanating from the hearth and entered the inner room. There, he found Bedovina lying alone in bed, her face serene but her words as scarce as autumn leaves. He called out to her, shaking her roughly, but she remained still, unwilling to speak for fear of shutting the door.

The servant boy's eyes roved over her tranquil form and, finding her comely despite her miserly nature, he lay down beside her. Though not well-prepared for his task, he managed to accomplish it, Bedovina remaining silent throughout, allowing him to do as he pleased while her husband watched from the bench.

When the servant boy departed, Bedovina arose and made her way to the entrance, where she found Sennuccio still awake. With a scolding tone, she rebuked him: "A fine husband you are! You've left me lying all night with the door wide open, inviting any rascal to enter our home, yet not lifting a hand to keep them at bay. You truly deserve to drink from a cup with a hole in it."

Sennuccio rose to his feet and retorted, "Now go ahead and shut the door, little fool that you are! I've outwitted you this time. You thought you'd make me shut the door, but now you're the one who's been tricked." Bedovina, realizing she had lost the wager, closed the door with a mixture of chagrin and amusement, then returned to bed beside her husband, who lay there with a knowing glint in his eye.
