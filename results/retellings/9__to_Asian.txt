In a bygone era, when the winds howled like a chorus of restless spirits and the rains lashed down upon the Cape of Good Hope, there existed a vessel from Batavia, bound for Table Bay. The captain, a seasoned sailor with a weathered face and a heart as tough as the sea itself, steered his ship through treacherous waters, but fate had other plans.

As the winter season descended upon the land, no vessel was allowed to enter the bay, lest it fall prey to the merciless guns of the batteries. The captain's ship, battered by the gale and tossed about like a toy on the waves, struggled to stay afloat. But alas, it was not meant to be.

The batteries fired their cannons, and the ship was forced to put to sea once more, leaving its crew to face the fury of the elements. The sailors say that this vessel has continued to beat about, lost at sea, until the day of judgment. Some claim to have seen it, a ghostly apparition on the horizon, with all sail set and its hull creaking under the strain.

I myself was present when His Majesty's ship Leven, under the command of Captain W.F.W. Owen, encountered this phantom vessel off Point Danger in 1823. The Leven was on its way from Algoa to Simon's Bay, and as it sailed through the darkness, a strange sight appeared on the horizon.

The Barracouta, a ship that should have been sailing in a different direction, was seen bearing down upon us with all sail set. Its rig was unmistakable, and soon we could make out the faces of its crew, gazing longingly towards our ship. Captain Owen attempted to close with her, but she stood away from us, as if beckoning us to follow.

As the sun dipped below the horizon, the Barracouta heaved to and lowered a boat into the water, apparently to rescue a man who had fallen overboard. But when night fell, there was no light to be seen, and we were left wondering if it was all just a trick of the mind.

The next morning, the Leven anchored in Simon's Bay, waiting anxiously for the Barracouta's arrival. When she finally appeared on the horizon, her log revealed that she had been 300 miles away from us when we thought we saw her. It seemed that no other vessel like hers had ever been seen around the Cape.

Years later, I found myself on a merchant ship, sailing through treacherous waters once more. The winds howled and the rains lashed down, just as they had all those years ago. As I stood on deck, recounting tales of the Flying Dutchman to my fellow passengers, the second officer suddenly pointed out to sea.

"Voila le volant Hollandais!" he exclaimed, his voice trembling with excitement. The captain sent for his night glass, and soon we could see a ship bearing down upon us with all sail set. Its rig was unmistakable, and people were visible on its poop, gazing longingly towards our ship.

The captain, a resolute Bordeaux mariner, shook his head in wonder. "It is quite incomprehensible," he muttered, as the stranger seemed to near us with the rapidity of lightning. But in an instant, it vanished into thin air, leaving us all staring at each other in confusion.

I give this account without remark, for I have seen it myself and know that it is true. And if you are a sailor, beware of the Flying Dutchman, for he will lure you to your doom with his ghostly ship and his beckoning crew.
