In the heart of our village, where the sun dips into the horizon and paints the sky with hues of crimson and gold, there lived a king with three sons: Pedro, Diego, and Juan. The king, wise and just, called his sons to him one day and said, "My children, I have decided that you shall set out from our kingdom and seek your fortunes. May the road rise up to meet you, and may the wind be at your back."

The three brothers took different paths, but before they parted ways, they agreed to meet in a certain place deep within the forest. After many days of walking, Don Juan came across an old man on the road. The old man gave Don Juan a loaf of bread and said, "Take this bread with you, but be sure to divide it among the monkeys guarding the gate to the palace when you arrive." Don Juan followed the old man's instructions, and as he entered the palace, he saw a big monkey.

The monkey called out to him, saying, "Don Juan, I know that your purpose in coming here was to find your fortune. And at this very moment, my daughter Chonguita will marry you." The archbishop of the monkeys was summoned, and Don Juan and Chonguita were married without delay.

A few days later, Don Juan asked permission from his wife to go to the place where he and his brothers had agreed to meet. Chonguita's mother, who had been watching over her daughter with love and care, said to Don Juan, "If you are going away, take Chonguita with you." And so, they set out together.

When Don Juan met his two brothers and their beautiful wives at the appointed place, he was overcome with shame. His brothers asked him where his wife was, and Don Juan replied, "She is behind me." When Pedro and Diego saw Chonguita, they were surprised and exclaimed, "What happened to you? Did you lose your head?" But Don Juan could say nothing.

At last, he broke out, saying, "Let us go home! Our father must be waiting for us." And so, the four of them began their journey back to the kingdom. Chonguita walked by her husband's side, and as they traveled, she proved herself to be a kind and gentle companion.

When the return of the three brothers was announced to the king, he hastened to meet them on the stairs. But when he learned that one of his sons had married a monkey, he fainted from shock. However, after he recovered his senses, he said to himself, "This misfortune is God's will. I must therefore bear it with patience."

The king assigned a house to each couple to live in, but the more he thought about it, the greater appeared to be the disgrace that his youngest son had brought upon the family. So one day, he called his three sons together and said, "Tell your wives that I want each one of them to make me an embroidered coat. The one who fails to do this within three days will be put to death."

The king issued this order in the hope that Chonguita would fail, but his hope was disappointed. On the third day, Pedro's wife and Diego's wife presented their coats, but Chonguita's was the prettiest of all.

Still anxious to get rid of the monkey wife, the king next ordered his daughters-in-law to embroider a cap for him in two days under penalty of death if they failed. But again, Chonguita proved herself to be a skilled and talented woman, and her cap was the most beautiful of them all.

At last, thinking of no other way by which he could accomplish his end, the king summoned his three daughters-in-law and said, "The husband of the one who shall be able to draw the prettiest picture on the walls of my chamber within three days shall succeed me on the throne." And so, the four women set to work.

When the three days were over, the king went to inspect their work. And when he saw Chonguita's picture, he was amazed by its beauty and skill. "This is indeed a masterpiece," he said. "Don Juan shall be my successor on the throne."

A great feast was held in the palace in honor of the new king. But as Don Juan danced with his wife, he became angry and hurled her against the wall. The hall suddenly grew dark, but after a while it brightened again, and Chonguita had been transformed into a beautiful woman.

As the night wore on, the guests marveled at the beauty of the new queen, and they said among themselves, "A true king is one who sees beyond appearances."
