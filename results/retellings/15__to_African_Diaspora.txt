In the heart of our village, where the forest whispers secrets to the wind, there lived a man who went into the woods to gather firewood for his family's hearth. As he walked, his eyes fell upon a serpent crushed beneath a massive stone. With a gentle touch, he raised the stone with the handle of his axe, and the snake slithered out, its body twisted in pain.

The serpent gazed at the man with cold, calculating eyes and hissed, "You have freed me from my prison, but now I shall feast on your flesh." The man replied calmly, "Let us first seek the counsel of a wise one. If I am condemned by their judgment, then you may eat me."

As they walked through the forest, they came upon a horse as thin as a reed, tied to an ancient oak tree. Its once-majestic body now hung limp and weak, its eyes sunken with hunger. The snake asked the horse, "Is it just for me to devour this man who has saved my life?" The horse's voice was barely audible, but his words cut deep: "More than just, it is right. Look at me! I once carried my master with pride and strength, but now I am nothing more than a shadow of my former self. Tied to this tree, I eat these few leaves and wait for the inevitable. Eat the man, then. For in our world, those who do good are ill rewarded, while those who do evil reap their just reward."

They continued on their journey until they stumbled upon an ancient mulberry tree, its branches gnarled with age. The snake asked the tree if it was right to eat the man who had saved its life. The tree's voice was like the rustling of leaves: "Yes, it is right. For I have given my master countless leaves, from which he has raised the finest silkworms in all the land. Now that I can no longer stand upright, he plans to cast me into the fire. Eat the man, then, for you will be doing a good deed."

As they walked further, they met a cunning fox with eyes as bright as the morning sun. The man took her aside and begged her to render judgment in his favor. The fox said, "To make my decision, I must see how this matter has unfolded." They returned to the spot where the snake lay beneath the stone, but when the man saw it again, he cried out, "Where you are, there I will leave you!" And so, the snake remained under the stone.

The fox, satisfied with her judgment, requested a payment: a bag of hens. The man promised to deliver them to her the next morning. But when the fox arrived at dawn, she found dogs instead of hens in the bag. The man warned her not to open it near the house, lest the mistress hear its contents. The fox, foolish and trusting, waited until they were far from home before opening the bag. Out came the dogs, and they devoured the cunning fox.

And so, our village elders say, "Who does good is ill rewarded, while who does evil reaps their just reward."
