In the heart of our village, where the ancestors watch over us, there lived a man who ventured into the forest to gather firewood for his family's evening meal. As he walked along the winding path, he chanced upon a serpent crushed beneath a massive stone. With the strength of his axe, he lifted the stone just enough for the snake to slither free.

The serpent, now unencumbered, gazed at the man with eyes that seemed to burn like embers. "You have freed me," it hissed, "and I will repay you in kind – by devouring your flesh." The man stood firm, his voice unwavering as he replied, "Let us seek the counsel of one who has witnessed the world's ways, for if I am condemned, then my fate is sealed."

Their first encounter was with a horse so gaunt it seemed to be a mere shadow of its former self. It had been tied to an ancient oak tree, its once-lustrous coat now dull and matted from years of neglect. The serpent asked the horse, "Is it just for me to devour this man who saved my life?" The horse's eyes clouded with sorrow as he spoke, "More than just – it is a matter of balance. I was once a proud steed, carrying our village elder on countless journeys. But now, abandoned and hungry, I will soon meet my demise. Eat the man, for in this world, good deeds go unrewarded, while evil actions are well-recompensed."

As they journeyed on, they came upon a mulberry tree whose branches were scarred from years of bearing fruit. The serpent asked its counsel, "Is it right to devour this man who saved my life?" The tree's voice was like the rustling of leaves as it replied, "Indeed, for I have given our village countless silkworms, and in return, I am cast aside – soon to be discarded into the fire. Eat the man, then, for you will be doing a noble deed."

Their final stop was at the den of a cunning fox. The man took her aside, pleading with her to speak on his behalf. The fox listened intently, her ears perked up like a pair of sharp horns. "Let me see how this matter has unfolded," she said, and together they returned to the spot where the serpent lay beneath the stone.

But as soon as the man saw the snake trapped once more, he cried out, "You will remain there, just as you are." And so it was – the snake remained under the stone. The fox, in payment for her services, requested a bag of hens from the man. He promised to deliver them to her the next morning.

The following day, the fox arrived at the agreed-upon spot, but instead of finding the promised hens, she discovered a bag filled with snarling dogs. The man had tricked her, and as she opened the bag, the dogs sprang free, devouring her in an instant. And so it is – those who do good are often ill-rewarded, while those who do evil prosper.

The villagers would say that this tale serves as a reminder of the world's ways: that kindness goes unrewarded, while malice is well-remunerated. But we must not forget that justice can be served in unexpected ways – and that sometimes, it is best to rely on one's own wit and cunning to ensure survival in a world governed by its own peculiar rules.
