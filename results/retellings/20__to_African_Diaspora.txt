In the days of our ancestors, when the rains poured down like a deluge from the heavens and the winds howled like a chorus of restless spirits, three brothers set out on a journey to hunt for sustenance. Their clothes were tattered, their bellies growling with hunger, but they pressed on, undeterred by the tempests that threatened to consume them.

As they walked, the brothers chanced upon a meadow, where a swift hare darted across their path. With guns in hand – though two were broken and one had no barrel – they took aim, firing wildly into the air. But the hare proved elusive, vanishing like a wisp of smoke on the wind.

"What shall we do?" cried one brother, his voice laced with desperation. "We must seek out our godmother," replied another, "for she is wise and kind, and will surely lend us aid in this time of need."

They knocked at her door, and though she was not at home, she answered their plea from afar. "My children," she said, "enter the kitchen and you shall find three pots, two broken and one with no bottom. Take whichever you wish." The brothers entered, choosing the pot without a bottom to cook their elusive hare.

As they waited for the hare to cook, one brother suggested they ask their godmother about her garden. She replied that she had three walnut trees – two dead and one barren of fruit – and invited them to take as many nuts as they pleased. But alas! The brother who shook the barren tree was rewarded with a nut that fell on his hat, breaking his heel.

Undeterred, they continued their journey, carrying the cooked hare in a salad basket. In a nearby village, where sickness had taken hold of many hearts, they posted a notice: "Whoever is hungry, come and receive broth in charity." The villagers flocked to partake, but one outsider drank too deeply from the skimmer, and fell into a deep slumber.

The brothers sought out three physicians – one blind, one deaf, and one dumb – to tend to their new friend. But these healers proved more hindrance than help. The blind man examined the tongue of the sleeping stranger, while the deaf man asked how he was faring. The dumb man scribbled on a piece of paper, "Go to the apothecary, buy two cents' worth of something unknown, and put it wherever you please." And with that, the physicians departed, leaving their patient in the care of the brothers.

As they watched over their friend, the brothers realized that sometimes, even the most well-meaning efforts can lead to chaos. But in the end, it was not the doctors or the godmother who held the key to healing – but rather the simple act of kindness and compassion shown by the brothers themselves.
