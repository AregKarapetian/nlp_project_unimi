In the days when the great king Brahmadatta ruled over Benares, a young lion emerged from his slumber, born of the Bodhisatta's spirit. He roamed the forest, strong and fierce, with eyes like shining stars on a clear night.

Near the Western Ocean, where palm trees swayed gently in the breeze, a humble hare lived beneath a vilva tree. One day, after feeding on the lush grass, he lay down to rest beneath the shade of his favorite palm sapling. As he gazed up at the leaves above, a thought struck him: "What would become of me if this earth were to crumble and shatter?" The very moment he pondered these words, a ripe vilva fruit fell onto a palm leaf with a loud thud.

The hare's heart skipped a beat as he leapt to his feet. He fled in terror, without looking back, convinced that the earth was indeed breaking apart. Another hare, who had been watching from afar, saw his companion's frantic flight and asked what drove him to such haste. "Don't ask me," the first hare replied, but the second hare pressed on, demanding an explanation.

The first hare finally relented, saying only, "The earth is collapsing." The second hare ran after him, followed by more hares, until a great host of animals – deer, boar, elk, buffalo, wild ox, rhinoceros, tiger, and elephant – joined the chase. They fled in terror, driven by the same fear that had gripped the hares.

As they ran, their numbers grew to fill an entire league. The Bodhisatta, observing this chaotic flight from a distance, realized that something was amiss. He thought to himself, "The earth is not crumbling; it must be some misinterpreted sound." With a fierce determination burning within him, he sprang forward with the speed of a lion and positioned himself before the fleeing animals.

He roared three times, his mighty voice echoing through the forest, causing the animals to halt in their tracks. They stood frozen, terrified by the sight of the powerful lion. The Bodhisatta approached them, asking why they fled in such panic. "The earth is collapsing," they replied. He pressed for more information, seeking out the elephants, who claimed that the lions knew the truth.

But the lions said it was the tigers' knowledge, and so on, until the deer finally admitted that they didn't know, but the hares did. The Bodhisatta turned to the hares, asking if it were true that the earth was breaking apart. One of them pointed to a particular hare and said, "He's the one who told us."

The Bodhisatta asked the accused hare about his claim, inquiring where he had seen the earth crumbling. The hare explained how he had been lying beneath the palm sapling when the vilva fruit fell onto the leaf, causing him to flee in terror. The lion listened carefully and thought to himself, "A ripe vilva fruit must have fallen on a palm leaf, creating a loud noise that this poor hare misinterpreted as the earth's collapse."

He reassured the animals, saying, "I will take this hare with me and find out if the earth is indeed crumbling." Placing the hare on his back, he set off towards the grove of palms and vilva trees. When they arrived, the Bodhisatta asked the hare to show him the exact spot where he had seen the earth shattering.

The hare hesitated, but the lion encouraged him to come closer. The hare stood afar off, pointing to a spot beneath the palm tree and crying out, "That's where I saw it!" He recited an ancient proverb: "When the wind blows through the trees, the leaves rustle with fear."

The Bodhisatta went to investigate, finding the ripe vilva fruit that had fallen onto the palm leaf. He realized that there was no truth to the hare's claim and gently placed him back on his back. Together they returned to the herd of animals, where the lion told them the whole story.

"Don't be afraid," he said. "The earth is not crumbling. It was just a misinterpreted sound." And with those words, the animals were finally reassured, free to roam and live without fear of destruction. The Bodhisatta's wisdom had saved them from certain doom, and they lived on, grateful for his protection.

As the sun set over the forest, the animals looked up at the stars twinkling in the night sky, knowing that they owed their lives to the great lion, born of the Bodhisatta's spirit.
