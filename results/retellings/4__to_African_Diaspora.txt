In a land far away, where the sun dipped into the horizon like a ripe mango in the sky, there lived a mighty king named Brahmadatta, ruler of Benares. But we are not here to speak of kings and thrones, for our tale is one of wisdom and compassion.

In a forest nearby, a young lion came into being, born from the earth itself, just as a lotus blooms in the mire. As he grew strong and fierce, he roamed the woods, king of beasts and lord of the wild. But little did he know that his greatest test was yet to come.

Near the Western Ocean, where palm trees swayed like gentle dancers in the breeze, there lived a humble hare. He spent his days lounging beneath the shade of a young palm sapling, at the foot of a vilva tree. One day, as he lay basking in the warmth, he thought to himself, "If this earth were to be destroyed, where would I flee?" And just then, a ripe vilva fruit fell on a palm leaf with a loud thud.

The hare's heart skipped a beat, and he leapt to his feet, thinking the earth was indeed crumbling beneath him. He fled in terror, without looking back, leaving behind his tranquil life. Another hare saw him running like a madman and asked, "What ails you, brother? Why do you flee with such haste?"

The first hare cried out, "Don't ask me! The earth is breaking apart!" But the second hare pressed on, saying, "Tell me, dear one, what's amiss?" And so the chase began. Hare after hare joined in, until a mighty host of animals – deer, boar, elk, buffalo, wild ox, rhinoceros, tiger, lion, and elephant – all took to flight together.

As they ran, their cries echoed through the forest, causing quite a commotion. But one wise creature watched from afar, observing the chaos with keen eyes. This was the Bodhisatta, future Buddha, who had been born as a young lion in the woods. He saw the panic-stricken animals and knew that something was amiss.

The Bodhisatta approached them with the speed of a lion, his mane flowing like a river in the wind. He roared three times, causing the animals to stop in their tracks, frozen with fear. The lion asked, "Why do you flee? What's this I hear about the earth breaking apart?" But the animals replied, "We don't know; we were told by the hares."

The Bodhisatta then questioned the elephants, but they said, "No, it was the lions who knew." And so the inquiry continued, with each group passing the buck to another. Finally, the deer pointed to a particular hare and said, "He's the one who told us."

The Bodhisatta asked the hare, "Is it true that the earth is breaking up?" The hare replied, "Yes, I saw it happen near the ocean, where I lived in a grove of palms mixed with vilva trees." But the lion was not convinced. He thought to himself, "A ripe vilva fruit must have fallen on a palm leaf, causing a loud thud. This hare jumped to conclusions and fled. I must find out the truth."

So the Bodhisatta reassured the animals, saying, "Do not be afraid. I will take this hare and go with him to the place he spoke of. Until I return, stay here." He placed the hare on his back and set off with great speed, leaving the herd behind.

When they arrived at the palm grove, the Bodhisatta asked the hare to show him the exact spot where he had seen the earth breaking apart. But the hare was afraid and stood afar off, pointing to a nearby vilva tree and saying, "Yonder is the place of dreadful sound." The lion then went to investigate, finding the ripe vilva fruit that had fallen on the palm leaf.

He discovered that it was just a simple accident, not a sign of the earth's demise. With this knowledge, he returned to the herd and told them the whole story, saying, "Do not be afraid; the earth is safe." And with his words, the animals were reassured, their fear dissipating like mist in the morning sun.

Thus, it was the Bodhisatta who saved the day, preventing a great calamity from befalling the animals. His wisdom and compassion had brought peace to the forest once more.
