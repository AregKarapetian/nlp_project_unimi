In the scorching desert, where the sun beats down like a drum on a ceremonial day, a hermit wandered, his feet carrying him to a place of refuge from the blistering heat. As he entered a vast cave, hidden from view by the dunes, his weary eyes beheld a sight that made his heart leap with wonder: a treasure trove of gold shone brightly in the darkness.

But the hermit's joy was short-lived, for he knew that such wealth often came with a price. He fled the cave as if Death itself were chasing him, and soon found himself face to face with three rough men who haunted the desert, preying on travelers like vultures on carrion. They lay in wait, their eyes gleaming with malice, but seeing no one pursuing the hermit, they grew wary.

"Tell us, old man," one of them growled, "what is it that chases you? Show us this Death you speak of."

The hermit hesitated, knowing that these men were not to be trusted. But their curiosity was piqued, and he saw no way to escape their questions. He led them back to the cave, pointing out the glittering gold as if it were a specter.

"Behold, my friends," he said, "the very thing I fled from. May God have mercy on us all."

The three men gazed upon the treasure in awe, congratulating themselves on their good fortune. But as they basked in their newfound wealth, their thoughts turned to how they might enjoy it for themselves.

"We must not leave this place until we've claimed every last coin," one of them said, his eyes gleaming with avarice.

But another countered, "Let us send one among us to the city to sell some of this treasure and bring back what we need. Then we can live like kings."

The devil's whisper seemed to stir within one of them, filling him with cunning and malice. He proposed that they divide the wealth, but secretly planned to poison his companions once he returned from the city.

As he made his way to the city, the treacherous man thought only of how he would soon be the sole owner of the treasure. But his companions, equally greedy, had their own plan: as soon as the poisoned food was consumed, they would strike down their unsuspecting friend and enjoy the wealth for themselves.

The treacherous one returned to the cave, bearing gifts of bread and wine, but also carrying a deadly poison in his heart. His companions devoured the tainted food, and soon fell dead, along with their betrayer.

Thus, the three men met their end, consumed by their own greed and deceit. The hermit, wise and cautious, had fled the cave, leaving the treasure untouched. And so it remained, a testament to the power of wisdom and the folly of those who seek to profit from evil.

As the sun set over the desert, casting long shadows across the sand, the people of the nearby village whispered among themselves about the fate of the three men. "May their story serve as a warning," they said, "for those who seek to harm others will ultimately find their own demise." And so, the legend of the three men and the cave of gold lived on, a cautionary tale told around campfires and in village squares for generations to come.
