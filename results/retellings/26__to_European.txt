In days of yore, when the land was parched and dry, and not a drop of water could be found, the creatures of the forest gathered 'neath the shade of an ancient tree. The lion, with his mighty roar, called forth the assembly to discuss the dire situation. "What shall we do?" he thundered, "for our rivers have run dry, and our springs have ceased to flow?"

The tiger, with his cunning eyes, proposed a plan: "Let us dance upon the riverbed, that we may tread out the water from beneath the earth!" The wolf, with his sharp teeth, snarled in agreement, while the jackal, with his wily grin, chimed in. Even the elephant, with her gentle trunk, joined in the chorus.

But one creature remained silent: Chonguita, the cunning rabbit. "Fools!" he exclaimed, "you will not succeed in this endeavor! I shall not join you in your foolish dance."

The other creatures laughed and jeered at Chonguita's words, but they danced on, their paws stomping out a rhythm upon the dry earth. And lo! The water began to rise, as if by magic, from beneath their feet.

Chonguita watched with a sly smile as his companions drank their fill, but he remained aloof, refusing to join in the revelry. "I shall drink your water," he taunted, "and you will not be able to stop me!"

The next morning, the creatures discovered Chonguita's footprints upon the riverbed, and they knew that he had indeed drunk from their waters. A great commotion arose among them, as they debated what to do with the cunning rabbit.

"Let us catch him!" cried the lion, but none could propose a plan. "I shall catch him," said old Tortoise, slowly advancing foot by foot. "Rub my shell with pitch, that I may resemble a stone, and Chonguita will step upon me, his feet sticking fast."

The creatures laughed at Tortoise's words, but they took him seriously, and soon his shell was covered in the sticky black wax. Tortoise lay down at the edge of the water, drawing his head into his shell, and waited for Chonguita to arrive.

As night fell, Chonguita came to drink from the river, and his eyes scanned the area until he spotted what he thought was a stone. "Ah ha!" he exclaimed, "they have placed a convenient rock for me to step upon!"

He trod upon Tortoise's shell with his left foot, but it stuck fast. Chonguita tried to shake it loose, but his right forefoot also became trapped. He stomped down with his hind foot, but that too remained stuck.

Chonguita used his head to hammer at Tortoise, and his tail as a whip, but both met the same fate as his feet. He was now fastened to the pitch, unable to move.

Tortoise slowly turned himself around, carrying Chonguita on his back, and made his way back to the other creatures. "Ha! ha! ha!" they laughed, "Chonguita, you are caught!"

The creatures debated what to do with the cunning rabbit, but he pleaded for a shameful death: "Take me by my tail and dash my head against a stone." They agreed, and it was decided that the lion should be the one to carry out this task.

Lion stood tall, his mane bristling with pride, as he grasped Chonguita's tail. But as he swung him around, the white skin slipped off from Chonguita's body, leaving nothing but a handful of hair and skin in Lion's paw. Chonguita was free once more, and the creatures laughed at their own foolishness.
