In days of yore, when the sun dipped into the horizon, casting a warm orange glow over the landscape, a cunning trickster named Hlakanyana chanced upon a young boy tending to his flock of goats. The boy was armed with a sturdy digging-stick, its wooden handle worn smooth by years of use.

Hlakanyana, ever on the lookout for an opportunity to test his wits against another, proposed that they join forces in pursuit of birds. The boy, naive and trusting, agreed to Hlakanyana's plan. Together, they chased after the feathered creatures, their footsteps echoing through the valleys and hills.

As the sun began its descent into the western sky, Hlakanyana suggested that they take a break by the riverbank. "Let us roast our birds," he said, his eyes glinting with mischief. The boy, still unaware of the trickster's true intentions, nodded in agreement.

But Hlakanyana had other plans. He proposed that they test their bravery by diving into the river and seeing who would emerge last. The boy, eager to prove himself, agreed to the challenge. Down they went, beneath the rippling waters, until only Hlakanyana remained submerged.

When he finally emerged, gasping for air, he accused the boy of cheating, claiming that he had eaten all the birds while still under water. The boy protested his innocence, but Hlakanyana would not be swayed. "You must pay for my birds with your digging-stick," he said, his voice dripping with malice.

The boy reluctantly handed over the digging-stick, and Hlakanyana continued on his journey. As he walked, he came across a group of potters, their hands stained with clay as they worked tirelessly to create vessels for the community. Hlakanyana approached them, a sly grin spreading across his face.

"Why do you not ask me to lend you this digging-stick?" he asked, holding out the boy's tool. "With it, you can dig and mix your clay with ease." The potters, unaware of the trickster's true nature, eagerly accepted his offer.

But as soon as they began to use the digging-stick, it broke in two, its wooden handle splintering into pieces. Hlakanyana laughed, a cold, mirthless sound. "You have broken my digging-stick," he said, his eyes glinting with triumph. "The one that I received from my companion, who ate my birds and left me with the heads."

The potters, realizing too late their mistake, offered Hlakanyana a pot in compensation for the broken tool. He accepted it, but not before warning them of its fate.

As he continued on his journey, Hlakanyana came across some boys tending to a flock of goats. "You foolish boys," he said, shaking his head in dismay. "Why do you not milk your goats into vessels? It is a waste to let their milk go to waste."

The boys, eager to please, asked Hlakanyana to lend them the pot he had received from the potters. He agreed, but as soon as they began to use it, the vessel shattered into pieces.

Hlakanyana laughed once more, his voice carrying across the landscape. "You have broken my pot," he said, his eyes glinting with satisfaction. "The one that I received from the people who made pots, who broke my digging-stick, which was given to me by my companion, who ate my birds and left me with the heads."

The boys, realizing their mistake, offered Hlakanyana a goat in compensation for the broken pot.
