In the misty mountains, where the sun dipped into the horizon, Hlakanyana chanced upon a young shepherd tending to his flock of goats. The boy was armed with a sturdy digging-stick, its wooden handle worn smooth by years of use. Hlakanyana proposed an impromptu hunt for birds, and the boy, eager for adventure, agreed.

Together they chased after the feathered creatures, their laughter echoing through the valleys as they traversed the rolling hills. As the day waned, the sun's fiery orb sinking below the treetops, Hlakanyana declared it was time to roast their catch. The pair arrived at a tranquil spot on the riverbank, where the gentle current whispered secrets to the reeds.

Hlakanyana suggested they dive into the water and see who would emerge last. The boy, trusting in his companion's words, agreed to this peculiar game. They plunged beneath the surface, their bodies rippling the water as they descended. Hlakanyana emerged first, leaving the boy to struggle free from the watery depths.

The cunning fellow proposed another attempt, and the boy, still breathless, nodded in agreement. Once more, they submerged themselves, but this time it was Hlakanyana who burst forth quickly, devouring their quarry while the boy remained underwater. When he finally emerged, he found only the skeletal remains of their catch.

Hlakanyana accused the boy of consuming their birds, citing his own emergence from the water as proof. The boy vehemently denied any wrongdoing, but Hlakanyana insisted that he must pay for the lost meal with the digging-stick. Reluctantly, the boy surrendered the tool to his companion.

As Hlakanyana continued on his journey, he encountered a group of artisans crafting clay vessels by the river's edge. He offered them the digging-stick, saying they might find it useful for their labor. The potters, grateful for the gift, accepted it with enthusiasm. However, in their haste, the tool snapped in two, its wooden handle splintering under the strain.

Hlakanyana retrieved the broken remains and pressed on, arriving at a clearing where boys were herding goats. He introduced himself to the young shepherds, remarking on their lack of foresight in not using a vessel to collect the milk from their charges. The boys, intrigued by his words, requested that he lend them a pot he possessed.

Hlakanyana handed over the clay container, but it shattered upon its first use, leaving the boys to face his wrath. He claimed that they had broken the very pot he received from the potters who had, in turn, destroyed his digging-stick. The boys, chastened, offered him a goat as compensation.

Hlakanyana's travels next led him to the keepers of calves, who were struggling to nurse their young charges. He proposed that they allow the calf to suckle first, thereby inducing the cow's milk to flow more freely. The keepers, eager for an end to their troubles, accepted his suggestion and took the calf into their care.

Tragically, the calf perished under their hands, leaving Hlakanyana to lament its loss once more. He accused the keepers of killing the calf, which had been given to them by the boys who tended goats, who in turn had broken his pot. The keepers, remorseful, presented him with a cow as reparation.

Undeterred, Hlakanyana continued on his way, eventually arriving at a settlement where cowherds were tending their charges. He observed that they neglected to allow the calf to suckle first, thereby preventing the cows from producing milk freely. The cowherds, recognizing the wisdom in his words, requested that he lend them the calf.

Hlakanyana agreed, but alas, the calf met its demise under their care. Once more, he claimed that the keepers of calves had killed the calf, which had been given to them by the boys who tended goats, who in turn had broken his pot, and so on, tracing the chain of events back to his companion who had eaten his birds.

The cowherds, chastened, presented him with a majestic bull as compensation. Hlakanyana, still unsatisfied, departed into the unknown, leaving behind a trail of broken tools and lost livestock in his wake.
