In a village nestled between two great rivers, there lived a humble farmer who toiled from dawn till dusk to tend to his land. One day, as he was working in the fields, a mighty lion appeared before him, its eyes blazing with an unholy hunger. The farmer trembled with fear, but he knew that he must not show it.

The lion demanded one of the farmer's oxen, threatening to slay both beasts and their master if his wish was not granted. The farmer, trying to placate the beast, handed over one of the oxen. That evening, he hastened into town and purchased another, so that come morning, he would be able to till the soil once more.

The next day, the lion returned, its roar thundering through the countryside as it claimed another ox from the farmer's herd. Again, the farmer surrendered one of his beasts, buying yet another to replace it. This pattern continued for several days, with the lion appearing each evening to demand an ox, and the farmer reluctantly handing over one.

One fateful eve, a cunning jackal chanced upon the farmer as he was driving home alone. The jackal's eyes sparkled with mischief as he inquired about the missing oxen. The farmer explained his predicament, telling of the lion's nightly visits and threats to slay them all if he did not comply.

The jackal listened intently, a plan forming in his mind. "I shall free you from this beast," he declared, "but first, promise me a sheep as payment." The farmer agreed, and the jackal outlined a clever ruse to deceive the lion.

Early the next morning, the farmer set out for the fields with two oxen by his side. As he worked, the lion appeared once more, demanding an ox. But just as it was about to strike, a deep voice boomed from the nearby hill, "Who speaks with you, farmer?" The lion cowered in fear, stammering that it was only speaking to God.

The farmer, undaunted, replied loudly, "It is but an Asko!" The voice on the hill bellowed back, "Then take your hatchet and split the block of wood." The lion, still trembling with fear, begged the farmer to strike gently. But instead, the farmer swung his hatchet with all his might, splitting the lion's skull in two.

The beast fell lifeless to the ground, and the jackal descended from the hill, a triumphant glint in his eye. "I have fulfilled my promise," he declared. "Now, as agreed, you shall give me a sheep."

The farmer hastened home, vowing to keep his word. He told his wife of the jackal's bravery and promised to reward him with a fine ram. But as she packed the animal into a leather sack, her eyes sparkled with mischief. She placed the sack in a wicker basket, alongside their loyal dog.

"Set the basket down in the field," she whispered to her husband, "and let fate decide what happens next." The farmer did as instructed, shouting out to the jackal that his ram was ready. But when the cunning fox approached the basket, the dog sprang up, chasing it away with a fierce bark.

The jackal fled, vowing never to aid men again. In the evening, the farmer returned to find the ram still intact in the basket. He picked it up, grinning from ear to ear, and declared that the jackal had failed to claim his prize. Together, they would feast on the succulent meat.

As they sat down to their meal, the farmer chuckled, "It seems the jackal has learned a valuable lesson: never trust a man's promises." And so, the tale of the cunning lion, the clever jackal, and the wise farmer was told and retold throughout the land, a cautionary story about the dangers of underestimating the wit and guile of those who live in the wild.
