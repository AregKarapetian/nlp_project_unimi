In days of yore, in a far-off kingdom, there lived a monarch who had mastered the art of understanding the language of beasts. He would often stroll through his gardens, surrounded by creatures great and small, and converse with them as if they were fellow nobles.

One sunny afternoon, as he walked among the fowl, he overheard their chirping praises for their benevolent king. "Our lord is most kind to us," they clucked. "He provides us with sustenance and refreshment." The monarch chuckled at their words, his eyes twinkling with amusement.

The queen, who had been strolling nearby, approached him and inquired, "What brings you such joy?" The king hesitated, knowing that if he revealed the true reason for his laughter, it would be a grave mistake. He replied, "I merely laughed at nothing, my dear."

The queen's curiosity was piqued, and she pressed him to explain himself. "There must be some reason behind your merriment," she said. "If you do not tell me, I fear for your life." The king, torn between his duty to keep the secret and his loyalty to his wife, decided that he could no longer conceal the truth.

He made his way to the gardens once more, where the animals were busy with their daily routines. As he watched them, a cock strutted forward, its feathers ruffled with importance. "Your Majesty," it said, "we have learned of your impending demise. Your queen has spoken words that have bound you to her will, and we fear for our own well-being if you do not comply."

The king laughed again at the cock's words, but this time his amusement was tinged with a hint of sadness. The queen approached him once more, her eyes narrowed in suspicion.

"What is it that amuses you now?" she asked. The king replied, "I was thinking of constructing a grand tank for our kingdom." The queen's face lit up with excitement, and she exclaimed, "What a wonderful idea! Let us bring the animals from Lankawa to help us build it."

The king, still hesitant, agreed to her proposal, and soon the creatures were gathered at the site. However, as they began to work, it became apparent that they were not equipped for such a task. A jackal, who had been watching from a distance, bounded forward with an air of nonchalance.

"Your Majesty," it said, "I have been observing the animals' efforts and noticed something peculiar." The king asked what he meant, and the jackal replied, "I was calculating the ratio of males to females in our kingdom. From my observations, I believe that the females are in excess."

The king, intrigued by the jackal's words, decided to investigate further. He returned home and consulted his records, only to discover that the jackal had been correct – there were indeed more males than females.

As he confronted the jackal with this information, it offered a clever explanation for its earlier claim. "Your Majesty," it said, "if one includes those males who are swayed by the words of their female counterparts, then the females would indeed be in excess."

The king was taken aback by the jackal's wisdom and decided to put an end to the project. He dismissed the animals, instructing them to return to the forest.

Meanwhile, the queen had been waiting anxiously for news from her husband. When she learned that he had abandoned the tank-building endeavor, she grew enraged. "What is the meaning of this?" she demanded. The king, his patience worn thin, took up a cane and began to chastise her.

The queen, realizing her mistake, begged for forgiveness and vowed never to question him again. And so, the monarch's secret was safe once more, thanks in part to the cunning and wit of the wise jackal.
