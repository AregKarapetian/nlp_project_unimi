In the village of Nkosi, where the sun dipped into the horizon and painted the sky with hues of crimson and gold, there lived a young man named Themba. He was a skilled herdsman, tending to his father's buffaloes with care and devotion. As he watched his animals graze in the open fields, he noticed a peculiar sight: every day at noon, a she-dog would make its way to a nearby ravine, where pools of water reflected the sky above.

Themba's curiosity was piqued, and he wondered who this dog belonged to and what it did in the ravine. He decided to observe from afar, hiding behind a bush as the dog approached the water. To his amazement, when the dog reached the pool, it shed its skin, revealing a beautiful maiden with skin as smooth as polished stone and hair as black as the night sky.

The maiden bathed in the pool, her movements fluid and carefree. When she finished, she donned the dog's skin once more, transforming back into the she-dog that Themba had first seen. The young man followed the dog to its home, a small hut on the outskirts of the village. He watched as it entered the house, and later inquired about its owner.

Themba's father and mother decided it was time for their son to marry, but Themba surprised them by announcing that he had made up his mind to have a dog for his wife. His family laughed at him, thinking him foolish, but Themba remained resolute. They concluded that he must indeed have the soul of a dog in him, and so they asked if there was any particular dog he would like to have as his bride.

Themba gave the name of the man whose house the she-dog visited daily. The master of the dog laughed at the idea but accepted a bride's price for her. A day was fixed for the wedding, and the ceremony took place in due form. The bride, still wearing the dog's skin, was escorted to Themba's house.

Every night, when her husband slept, the bride would slip out of the dog's skin and roam free. But one evening, Themba pretended to sleep, watching as she prepared to leave. He caught hold of her and seized the dog's skin, throwing it into the fire where it was reduced to ashes. The maiden remained with him, but her beauty was now more radiant than ever.

Word spread quickly through the village about Themba's wisdom in marrying a dog. His friend Jitu, who had been watching from afar, thought he could do no better than marry a dog himself. His relations made no objection, and a bride was selected. The marriage ceremony took place, but when they applied vermilion to the bride's forehead, she began to growl.

In spite of her growling, Jitu's family dragged her to his house, where they anointed her with oil and turmeric. But as soon as the bride's party set off towards their home, the dog broke free and chased after them. The villagers shouted for Jitu to run after his bride, but she only growled and bit at him.

Jitu, humiliated and ashamed, eventually gave up the chase. His friends laughed so heartily that he was too embarrassed to speak for days. Two or three days later, in a fit of despair, he hanged himself from a tree branch. The villagers whispered among themselves about Jitu's fate, cautioning their children against the dangers of meddling with forces beyond human control.

From that day on, Themba and his bride lived happily together, surrounded by the beauty and wonder of the natural world. Their love story became a legend, told and retold around campfires, reminding all who listened of the power of perseverance and the importance of respecting the balance between humans and animals in the world they shared.
