In a small village nestled between two great rivers, there lived a devoted couple, Anansi and his wife, Akua. One sunny afternoon, as they sat beneath their thatched roof, Anansi said to Akua, "Let us prepare some delicious fritters for our evening meal." Akua replied, "But what shall we use to fry them? We have no pan of our own."

"Go and borrow one from my mother," Anansi suggested. "It is only a short walk away." But when Akua returned with the borrowed pan, she said to her husband, "You must take it back to your mother's house when we are finished cooking." And so they prepared their meal.

After eating, Anansi proposed a challenge: "Let us both go to work and see who speaks first. The one who breaks silence shall carry this pan back to my mother." Akua began to spin the thread for her weaving, while Anansi sat quietly, drawing his own thread as he worked on a new pair of shoes.

As they labored in silence, a soldier with a majestic horse rode by, seeking a skilled shoemaker. A nearby woman pointed out Anansi's house and introduced him to the soldier. The man asked for help cutting a girth for his horse, promising payment. But when Anansi remained silent, only responding with "Leulerò, leulerò," the soldier grew impatient.

"You must come and cut my horse's girth, or I will take your life!" he threatened. Still, Anansi refused to speak first, repeating only "Leulerò, leulerò." His wife, Akua, chimed in with "Piciciì, picicì, piciciò," as she continued spinning her thread.

The soldier's anger reached a boiling point, and he seized his sword, ready to strike. But Anansi remained steadfast, unwilling to be the first to break silence. Finally, when the soldier's rage was at its peak, Akua cried out in distress, "Ah! Don't take his life!" And with that, Anansi exclaimed, "Now you go and carry the pan back to my mother, and I will attend to your horse!"

With a smile, he took up the task, and proved himself the victor once more. As they returned home, Akua turned to her husband and said, "You are indeed a clever one, Anansi. Your wisdom is as vast as the rivers that flow through our village."
