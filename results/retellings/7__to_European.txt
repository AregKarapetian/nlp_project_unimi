In days of yore, when darkness fell upon the land, an elderly woodsman was beset by a tempest's fury as he trudged homeward, burdened with faggots for the fire. Seeking refuge from the torrential rain and flashes of lightning, he stumbled upon a hollow tree, its ancient bark twisted and gnarled with age. As the storm subsided, the woodsman cautiously emerged to find himself in the midst of a strange and wondrous spectacle.

A company of demons, their faces aglow with an otherworldly light, capered and danced beneath the moon's silver rays. Their antics were so fantastical that the woodsman, his caution forgotten, burst into laughter, and soon found himself standing among them, watching in rapt attention as they twirled and leaped with abandon.

Among this merry throng was one demon who danced with a clumsiness that brought a smile to the woodsman's lips. The leader of the company, a towering figure with eyes that glowed like embers, gazed upon his fellow's antics with displeasure. "Enough!" he cried, his voice like thunder in the stillness. "Is there none among you who can dance better than this wretched creature?"

The woodsman, it seemed, had been transported back to a bygone era when he was a master of the dance. Summoning forth the memories of his youth, he offered to demonstrate his skills before the demons. And so, with steps light and agile, he danced beneath the moon's watchful gaze.

The demons, entranced by the woodsman's performance, applauded and cheered, offering him a cup of enchanted sak that granted him strength and vitality. They begged him to dance again, and the woodsman, basking in their admiration, readily complied.

As the night wore on, the leader of the demons approached the woodsman with a mischievous glint in his eye. "You have danced for us tonight," he said, "but you must leave behind a token of your visit. That wen upon your right cheek shall be our pledge." With a deft touch, the demon removed the wen, and as it vanished into thin air, the demons vanished with it.

The woodsman, still feeling the warmth of their applause, stumbled homeward, his hand tracing the smooth skin of his cheek. His wife, waiting anxiously by the fire, was overjoyed to see her husband's return, and listened in wonder as he recounted his fantastical encounter with the demons.

But not all were pleased by the woodsman's good fortune. A wicked and cantankerous old man, who dwelled in a neighboring cottage, had long been afflicted with a wen on his left cheek. Enraged by the woodsman's success, he called upon him, feigning friendship and curiosity.

The woodsman, unaware of the old man's true intentions, told him where to find the hollow tree, advising him to hide within its ancient boughs just before sunset. The wicked old man, his heart filled with malice, crept into the tree, but as he watched the demons dance, he was seized by a sudden urge to join in.

With a flourish of his fan, he began to dance, but alas! His movements were clumsy and awkward, causing the demons to express their displeasure. "You dance extremely ill," one of them said, "and the sooner you stop, the better we shall be pleased." And with that, they flung the wen at the old man's right cheek, where it remained firmly fixed.

The wicked old man, his face now marred by two wens, slunk away in defeat, while the woodsman and his wife returned to their humble cottage, their hearts filled with joy and wonder.
