In a small village nestled in the mountains, there lived two sisters, one as virtuous as a lotus flower and the other as wicked as a serpent. The virtuous sister said one day to her mother: "I will go and fetch water from the well." But the wicked one refused to obey their mother's command.

The virtuous sister went to draw the water, but the bucket fell into the well. She thought to herself: "If I return home without the bucket, my mother will surely scold me." So she climbed down the well, her heart filled with determination. At the bottom of the well, she found a narrow passage leading to three doors.

She knocked on the first door and asked: "Have you not seen a cord and bucket?" A wise old sage replied: "No, my child." The virtuous sister continued on her journey and knocked on the second door. Again, she asked: "Have you not seen a cord and bucket?" This time it was the devil who answered, his voice dripping with malice. But the virtuous sister did not falter; she knew that he was not to be trusted.

She knocked on the third door and asked once more: "Have you not seen a cord and bucket?" This time, it was the benevolent Goddess of Mercy who replied: "Yes, my child. Listen carefully. I have a young son here who needs his soup; would you please give it to him? And when I return, I will reward you with your bucket." The virtuous sister agreed and gave the child his broth. She then swept the house, but instead of finding dirt, she discovered beautiful coral and other treasures.

The Goddess of Mercy returned and asked: "Have you done as I instructed?" The virtuous sister replied: "Yes, but I kept these treasures aside to give to you when you return." The Goddess smiled and said: "Keep them for yourself. Would you like a dress made from the finest silk or one woven from calico threads?" The virtuous sister chose the calico dress, but the Goddess gave her the silk one instead.

"Would you prefer a brass thimble or a silver one?" the virtuous sister asked. But again, the Goddess surprised her by giving her the silver thimble. "Here is your bucket and cord," she said. "When you leave this passage, look up at the sky." The virtuous sister did as instructed, and a brilliant star fell upon her forehead.

She returned home to find her mother waiting for her, scolding her for being away so long. But when her mother saw the star shining on her daughter's brow, she was overcome with wonder. "Where have you been?" she asked, but the virtuous sister replied: "I do not know what this is."

The wicked sister, who had been watching from afar, grew envious of her sister's good fortune. She decided to follow in her footsteps and climbed down the well, knocking on each door in turn. But when she reached the Goddess's door, she was tempted by the devil's words: "Come here, my child; I will give you a reward."

The wicked sister succumbed to her own desires and ate the broth meant for the young son. She swept the house but found only dirt and dust. When the Goddess returned, she asked: "Have you done as I instructed?" The wicked sister lied, saying that she had given the child his soup and swept the house.

The Goddess gave her a choice between two dresses, but the wicked sister chose the wrong one. She was given the brass thimble instead of the silver one, and when she left the passage, a lump of dirt fell upon her forehead, soiling her face.

The virtuous sister's mother saw the contrast between her two daughters and understood that the Goddess had intervened to teach them a lesson. "I see now," she said, "that I have favored the wicked one and neglected the good one."
