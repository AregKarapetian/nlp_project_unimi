In a small village nestled between two great mountains, there lived a young woman named Mei. She was known throughout the village for her kindness and obedience to her mother. One day, her mother instructed her to draw a bucket of water from the well, but Mei's sister, the bad one, refused to help.

Mei, being the good daughter that she was, volunteered to complete the task. As she made her way to the well, the bucket slipped from her grasp and fell into the depths below. Fearing her mother's wrath, Mei decided to climb down after it. The air grew thick with mist as she descended deeper into the well.

At last, she reached a narrow passage and came upon a door. She knocked gently, calling out, "Have you not found a cord and bucket?" A wise old sage replied from within, "No, my child." Mei continued on her way, encountering another door where a mischievous spirit answered with a growl, "No, I have not found them; but come here, my child, come here."

Mei's heart remained steadfast, and she knocked at the next door. A kind voice replied, "Have you not found a cord and bucket?" It was the benevolent Bodhisattva who spoke to her from within. "I am going away," she said. "You will give my young son his soup, and then you will sweep and put the house in order. When I return, I will give you your bucket."

Mei agreed to help and spent the day caring for the Bodhisattva's child and tidying up the house. As she swept, her broom revealed hidden treasures: coral, jade, and other precious stones. She set them aside, intending to present them to the Bodhisattva upon her return.

When the Bodhisattva returned, Mei reported that she had completed all tasks as instructed. The Bodhisattva smiled and said, "Very well; keep these treasures for yourself. Would you like a dress of silk or one of calico?" Mei replied, "No, no! I prefer the calico." But the Bodhisattva surprised her by giving her the silk dress instead.

"Would you like a brass thimble or a silver one?" Mei asked. The Bodhisattva chuckled and said, "No, take the silver thimble." And to Mei's surprise, she handed over the silver one. "Here is your bucket and cord," she said. "When you reach the end of this passage, look up into the air."

Mei followed the Bodhisattva's instructions and gazed upwards. A brilliant star descended upon her brow, shining brightly for all to see. She returned home, where her mother scolded her for being away so long. But when she saw the star on Mei's forehead, she was taken aback.

"Where have you been until now?" her mother asked. "Who put that thing on your forehead?" Mei replied innocently, "I don't know what there is there." Her mother tried to wash it away, but the star only shone brighter. Then Mei told her tale of woe and wonder, and her mother understood that the Bodhisattva had bestowed a great blessing upon their family.

Mei's sister, the bad one, was envious of her sibling's good fortune. She begged to follow in Mei's footsteps and descended into the well, seeking out the cord and bucket. At first, she met with the wise old sage and the mischievous spirit, but when she knocked at the Bodhisattva's door, she was welcomed.

The Bodhisattva instructed her as she had done with Mei: to care for her son and sweep the house. But instead of doing as she was told, the bad girl ate the soup meant for the child and swept up only dirt. When the Bodhisattva returned, she asked if all tasks had been completed.

The bad girl lied, saying that she had done everything as instructed. The Bodhisattva smiled knowingly and said, "Do you wish the brass or silver thimble?" The bad girl eagerly chose the silver one, but received the brass instead. "Would you like a silk dress or a calico one?" she asked, to which the Bodhisattva replied, "Take the calico dress."

When the bad girl emerged from the passage, she looked up into the air as instructed. But instead of a star, a lump of dirt fell upon her face, soiling her entire visage. She stormed home in anger, scolding Mei for her good fortune and weeping tears of bitterness.

Her mother tried to wash away the dirt, but it only seemed to spread. Then she realized that the Bodhisattva had bestowed this punishment upon their family, revealing a truth: that they had favored the bad girl over the good one.
