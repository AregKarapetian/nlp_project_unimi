In the village of Azizi, where the sun dips into the savannah, a farmer's wife was busy winnowing corn, her hands moving in rhythm with the wind. A crow, its feathers as black as the night sky, swooped down and snatched a grain from the basket, flying off to perch on a nearby acacia tree.

The farmer's wife's face turned red with rage, like the embers of a fire that refuses to be extinguished. She flung a clod at the crow, striking it with such precision that the bird tumbled to the ground, dropping the grain into a narrow crack in the tree trunk.

The farmer's wife rushed up to the crow, grasping its tail and demanding, "Give me back my grain of corn! If you don't, I'll wring your neck like a chicken at slaughter!" The crow, trembling with fear, promised to retrieve the grain. But when it returned, it found that the grain had rolled too far into the crack for even its sharp beak to reach.

The crow flew off to seek help from the village elder, who said, "I will not cut down the tree, for it is a sacred one." The crow continued on to the king's palace, where the monarch refused to order the execution of an innocent man. Next, the crow visited the queen, but she would not persuade her husband to change his mind.

The crow flew further still, meeting a snake who refused to bite the queen, and a stick that refused to beat the snake. It encountered fire that refused to burn the stick, water that refused to quench the flames, an ox that refused to drink the water, and a rope that wouldn't bind the ox.

Finally, the crow met a mouse, which was too small to gnaw through the rope. But when it heard the name of its arch-nemesis, a cat, it knew its fate was sealed. The cat pounced on the mouse with lightning speed, proving that even in the face of adversity, some things are as certain as the rising sun: a cat will always catch a mouse.
