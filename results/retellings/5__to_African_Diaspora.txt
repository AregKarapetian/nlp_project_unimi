In the village of Azizi, where the sun dips into the horizon like a ripe mango, there lived a farmer's wife named Akua. She was winnowing corn on the outskirts of the village, her hands moving in rhythm with the wind, when a crow swooped down and snatched a grain from the basket. The bird perched on a nearby tree, its black feathers glistening like polished ebony.

Akua's face turned as red as the earthy soil she tilled, and she hurled a clod at the crow with such precision that it fell to the ground, dropping the grain into a narrow crack in the tree. The crow, frightened by Akua's aim, promised to return the grain, but when it came back, the grain had rolled too far into the crack for even its sharp beak to retrieve.

The crow flew off to seek help from the wise elder of the village, who refused to intervene. Undeterred, the bird continued on to the palace of the king, who sat on his throne like a mighty baobab tree, unmoved by the crow's plea. The king sent the crow to the queen, who smiled sweetly and said she would not coax her husband into killing the man.

The crow flew on, meeting a snake in the forest, which refused to bite the queen. It met a stick, which refused to beat the snake; a fire, which refused to burn the stick; water, which refused to quench the flames; an ox, which refused to drink from the water; and a rope, which refused to bind the ox.

As the crow flew on, it encountered a mouse, who laughed at the idea of gnawing through the rope. But when the crow mentioned the name "cat," the mouse's laughter turned to terror, for in Azizi, everyone knows that a cat will not rest until she has caught her prey. And so, the crow met its match in a sleek black cat, who pounced on the mouse with lightning speed.
