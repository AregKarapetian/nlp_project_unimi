The village elder sat on a mat, surrounded by the community's children, and began to tell a tale of two sisters.

In a small hut not far from here, lived a mother with three daughters. One day, she sent her youngest daughter, Chonguita, to fetch water from the well. But instead of going herself, the eldest sister, Akua, refused to obey their mother's command. Chonguita, however, took responsibility and went to draw the water.

As she was walking back with the bucket, it slipped from her hands and fell down the well. She thought to herself, "If I go home now without the bucket, who knows what my mother will do to me?" So, she climbed down into the well, determined to retrieve it.

At the bottom of the well, Chonguita found a narrow passage with three doors. She knocked on the first door and asked if they had seen her bucket. A wise old saint replied, "No, child." But when she knocked on the second door, the devil answered with anger in his voice, not even calling her "child."

Chonguita did not let this deter her. She continued to the third door and found the Madonna, who said, "Yes, my child. I have seen your bucket. Listen, I need a favor from you. While I'm away, take care of my little son, give him his soup, sweep the house, and put everything in order. When I return, I'll give you back your bucket."

The Madonna left, and Chonguita took on her tasks. She gave the child his broth, swept the floor, and instead of finding dirt, she discovered beautiful coral and other treasures. She set them aside to give to the Madonna when she returned.

When the Madonna came back, she asked Chonguita if she had done as she was told. Chonguita replied that she had, but also kept the treasures she found. The Madonna smiled and said, "Very well, keep them for yourself." She then offered Chonguita a choice between two dresses – one of calico and one of silk. Chonguita chose the calico dress, but the Madonna gave her the silk one.

The Madonna also asked Chonguita if she wanted a brass or silver thimble. Chonguita chose the brass one, but the Madonna gave her the silver one. Finally, she handed Chonguita her bucket and cord, saying, "When you leave this passage, look up into the air."

Chonguita did as instructed and a beautiful star fell onto her forehead. She returned home to find her mother waiting for her, ready to scold her for being away so long. But when she saw the star on Chonguita's brow, she was amazed.

Akua, the eldest sister, heard about the star and wanted to experience it for herself. She let the bucket fall down the well and climbed down to retrieve it. At the bottom, she knocked on the first door and asked if they had seen her bucket. The saint replied, "No, child."

Akua continued to the second door and found the devil. He answered with anger in his voice, but Akua refused to be deterred. She knocked on the third door and found the Madonna.

The Madonna said, "Yes, my child. I have seen your bucket. Listen, I need a favor from you. While I'm away, take care of my little son, give him his broth, sweep the house, and put everything in order. When I return, I'll give you back your bucket."

But Akua did not follow the Madonna's instructions. She ate the child's broth herself and swept the floor, finding only dirt. The Madonna returned and asked Akua if she had done as told.

Akua replied that she had, but the Madonna saw through her lies. "Do you want a brass or silver thimble?" she asked. Akua chose the silver one, but the Madonna gave her the brass one. "Do you want a calico dress or a silk one?" Akua chose the silk dress, but the Madonna gave her the calico one.

The Madonna handed Akua her bucket and cord, saying, "When you leave this passage, look up into the air." But when Akua did as instructed, instead of a star falling onto her forehead, a lump of dirt fell, soiling her face.

Akua returned home in anger, scolding Chonguita for having the star while she had only dirt on her face. Their mother tried to wash away the dirt, but it only seemed to spread. She realized that the Madonna had done this to show her that she loved Akua more than Chonguita.

The villagers nodded in understanding, and the elder continued, "This story teaches us that good deeds are rewarded, while bad ones are punished. May we all strive to be like Chonguita, who showed kindness and obedience."
