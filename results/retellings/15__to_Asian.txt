In a small village nestled between two great mountains, there lived a humble woodcutter named Kaito. One day, while gathering firewood in the forest, he stumbled upon a serpent crushed beneath a massive boulder. With gentle care, Kaito lifted the stone with his axe handle, and the snake slithered free.

As the serpent regained its strength, it gazed at Kaito with malevolent intent and hissed, "I shall devour you." Kaito replied calmly, "Let us first seek the counsel of a wise being. If I am condemned, then you may eat me as your due."

Their search led them to a skeletal horse tied to an ancient oak tree. The horse's eyes were sunken, its coat dull and matted. The serpent asked, "Is it just for me to devour this man who saved my life?" The horse replied, "Indeed, it is more than just. Look at me! I was once a proud steed, carrying my master through many seasons. Now, I am nothing but a shadow of my former self. Tied to this tree, I wither away, waiting for the inevitable. Eat the man, then. For those who do good are ill rewarded, and those who do evil are well rewarded."

As they continued their journey, they came upon a mulberry tree, its branches gnarled and hollowed out by age. The serpent inquired, "Is it right to devour this man who saved my life?" The ancient tree responded without hesitation, "Yes, for I have given my master countless leaves, which he has used to raise the finest silkworms in the land. Now that I am no longer useful, he plans to cast me into the fire. Eat the man, then, and do a good deed."

Their next encounter was with a cunning fox. Kaito took her aside and begged for her judgment. The fox said, "To render a fair verdict, I must see how matters stand." They returned to the spot where they first met, and as soon as Kaito saw the snake crushed beneath the boulder, he cried out, "There you shall remain!" And so it was.

The fox, pleased with her payment of a bag of hens, departed. But when she arrived at the designated place, Kaito had secretly filled the bag with dogs instead. He warned her not to open it near the village, lest the mistress of the house hear the commotion. The fox, eager for her prize, waited until they were far from human ears before opening the bag. To her dismay, the dogs burst forth and devoured her.

Thus, it is said that in this world, those who do good are often ill rewarded, while those who do evil prosper.
