In the village of Akwaba, where the sun dipped into the horizon like a ripe mango, Sennuccio and his wife Bedovina sat on their porch, sipping sweet tea under the shade of a baobab tree. The air was heavy with the scent of blooming flowers and the sound of crickets provided a soothing melody.

Sennuccio, a man known for his laziness, said to his wife, "Bedovina, shut the door, for now it's time for us to retire." But Bedovina, a woman as stubborn as a goat on a rocky terrain, replied, "You do it yourself, Sennuccio. I'm not about to lift a finger."

The two of them sat there, bickering like a pair of old tortoises, neither willing to give in. As the night wore on, Sennuccio suggested, "Let's make a bargain, Bedovina. Whoever speaks first will shut the door." His wife agreed, and they both sat back, waiting for the other to crack.

But as the hours ticked by, Bedovina grew tired of the game and her eyelids began to droop like a ripe plantain. She got up from her seat, took off her clothes, and slipped into bed, leaving Sennuccio sitting on the porch, still waiting for his wife to speak first.

Just then, a young servant boy passed by, carrying a lantern with a flickering candle. The flame went out, and he knocked on the door, calling out, "Is anyone inside? I need some light for my candle." But Sennuccio remained silent, too afraid to speak up lest he lose the wager.

The servant boy entered the house, looking around until he spotted Sennuccio sitting on the porch. He approached him gently, saying, "Good brother, what's wrong with you? Why won't you answer me?" But Sennuccio kept his mouth shut, still waiting for Bedovina to speak first.

The servant boy continued on into the house, where he found a faint light coming from the hearth. He entered the inner room and spotted Bedovina lying in bed, fast asleep. He shook her gently, but she refused to stir, too afraid to speak up lest she lose the wager.

The servant boy, taken by Bedovina's beauty, decided to stay with her for a while. Despite his own fatigue, he managed to accomplish what needed to be done, all without a word from Bedovina. And when he left, Bedovina got out of bed and went to shut the door, only to find Sennuccio still sitting on the porch.

She chastised him, saying, "You're a fine husband, indeed! You've left me lying here with the door wide open, vulnerable to any lewd fellow who might come in. You should be made to drink from a calabash with a hole in it!" Sennuccio rose to his feet, grinning mischievously, and said, "Ah, now you're the one who's been tricked! I'm equal with you now. You thought you'd win this wager, but it seems you've lost."

Bedovina realized she had indeed lost the game, but she also knew that she had enjoyed a merry night with the young servant boy. With a mixture of shame and amusement, she shut the door and went back to bed, her husband by her side.

As they lay there in the darkness, Sennuccio whispered to his wife, "You see, my dear, sometimes it's better to speak up than to wait for someone else to do so." Bedovina smiled to herself, knowing that she had learned a valuable lesson that night.
