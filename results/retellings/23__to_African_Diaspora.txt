In the village of Azizi, where the sun dips into the horizon and paints the sky with hues of crimson and gold, there lived a couple, Sennuccio and Bedovina. One evening, as they sat near the entrance of their hut, basking in the warmth of the season, Sennuccio said to his wife, "Bedovina, it is time for us to retire, shut the door." But she replied, "You may shut it yourself, my husband."

The dispute continued, with neither willing to concede. Sennuccio proposed a wager: "Let us make a bargain, Bedovina. The one who speaks first shall shut the door." His wife, known for her stubbornness and laziness, agreed to this proposal.

As they sat in silence, the air grew thick with anticipation. The village elder would often say, "A quiet tongue is like a still river, but it can also be as treacherous as a swollen one." Sennuccio's wife, however, soon found herself succumbing to the weight of her eyelids, and she slipped away to bed.

As fate would have it, a young servant from the nearby estate passed by their hut. His lantern's candle had gone out, and he sought refuge in Sennuccio's open door. "Is anyone within?" he called out, but his voice was met with silence. The servant entered further into the house, where he found Sennuccio sitting on a bench, his eyes wide open.

"Good brother, what ails you? Answer me quick," the servant said, gently shaking him awake. But Sennuccio remained mute, holding his tongue due to fear of being punished for speaking first and thus shutting the door himself. The servant then ventured into the inner room, where he spotted Bedovina lying in bed.

He approached her quietly, calling out softly, but she refused to respond, fearing that if she spoke, she would be forced to shut the door herself. The young servant, taken by her beauty and silence, decided to take matters into his own hands. He lay down beside her, and despite their awkwardness, he managed to complete his task.

When the servant departed, Bedovina rose from bed and made her way to the entrance of the hut. There she found Sennuccio still sitting on the bench, wide awake. She chided him, saying, "You have left me vulnerable, my husband! Any lewd fellow could have entered our home while you sat idle, doing nothing to protect us."

Sennuccio rose from his seat and retorted, "Now it is your turn, Bedovina. You thought you had outsmarted me, but I have won the wager. You must shut the door now." His wife realized she had indeed lost the bet and that her silence had led to a night of unexpected pleasure.

With a mix of embarrassment and resignation, Bedovina shut the door and joined Sennuccio in bed. As they lay together, the village elder's words echoed in their minds: "A quiet tongue can be as treacherous as a swollen river."
