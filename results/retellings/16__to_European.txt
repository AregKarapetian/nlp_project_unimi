In the village nestled beside a tranquil tank, a cunning crocodile had made its lair in the embankment. However, as the mud dried and hardened, the beast found itself trapped, unable to escape. Its fate seemed sealed, for it would soon succumb to the unforgiving earth.

As fate would have it, a traveler passed by on his way to fetch a midwife to attend to his wife's impending birth. The crocodile, desperate for rescue, called out to the man, "Kind sir, save me from this predicament! Break up the earth and set me free!"

The traveler, moved by the creature's plea, obliged and broke open the hardened mud, releasing the crocodile. Yet, as he placed the beast on his shoulder to transport it to the nearby river, the crocodile's intentions turned treacherous. Seizing the man's arm, it prepared to devour him.

"Why would you eat me after I saved your life?" the man asked, incredulous. "Do you not recall how I freed you from this very spot?"

The crocodile replied, "Indeed, I am grateful for your kindness, but hunger has consumed my heart. I must eat."

The man, resigned to his fate, proposed a condition: "Very well, I shall accept my demise, but first, let us seek the opinions of two or three witnesses."

Together, they journeyed to gather the testimony of various creatures. The first witness was a majestic kumbuk tree, whose bark and leaves were often pilfered by the man for his own purposes.

"O crocodile," said the tree, "do not spare this wretched soul. He is a thief, who steals from me in the dead of night, stripping my branches bare. His wickedness knows no bounds."

The next witness was a gentle cow, whose milk was often taken by the man without compensation or gratitude.

"O crocodile," said the cow, "do not let this man go unpunished. He has drained our udders dry and ultimately slaughtered us for his own sustenance. His cruelty is unmatched in all the land."

Finally, they came upon a wise jackal, who listened intently to the tale before rendering its verdict.

"I must know more about this affair," said the jackal, "before I can render judgment." The man, with the crocodile still perched on his shoulder, led the jackal back to the burrow and recounted the entire story. The jackal's demeanor changed from curiosity to indignation as it observed the man's wickedness.

"Fear not, good sir," said the jackal. "I am on your side. Take up a cudgel and strike him down, for I have witnessed his iniquity firsthand."

The man, emboldened by the jackal's words, seized a club and dealt the crocodile a crushing blow. The beast lay defeated, its wicked intentions foiled once more by the cunning of the traveler.
