In the village of Azizi, where the sun dipped into the horizon like a fiery ember, a poor but proud farmer and his wife sat by the fire, their bellies full from a meager supper. The air was thick with the scent of burning wood and simmering emotions as they bickered over who should shut the door that had swung open in the gusty wind.

"Who speaks first gets to close it," said the farmer, his voice firm but tinged with a hint of desperation.

The wife's eyes flashed like the stars on a clear night. "I will not be silenced," she retorted, her words dripping with conviction.

As they sat there, refusing to yield, the wind howled and the shadows danced on the walls. The old couple retired to their bed, still arguing in silence.

But as the night wore on, the sound of scratching and crunching echoed through the room. A wild dog had slipped in, its eyes gleaming like lanterns in the dark, as it devoured the family's meager store of food.

Still, neither the farmer nor his wife spoke a word, their faces set like stone. The dog finished its feast and departed, leaving behind a trail of destruction and a silence that hung heavy as a fog.

The next morning, the woman set out to grind grain at her neighbor's mill, leaving the farmer alone in the house. A barber chanced upon him, his eyes wide with wonder at the sight of the man's silent form.

"What sorcery is this?" the barber exclaimed, but the farmer remained mute, his face a mask of stone.

The barber shaved off half the farmer's beard and mustache, then coated him in lampblack, but still the man did not speak. "He must be bewitched!" cried the barber, fleeing in haste.

Just as the barber vanished into the crowd, the wife returned from her errand, her eyes scanning the room with a mixture of curiosity and alarm. And that was when she saw it: her husband, transformed by his own silence, his face a ghastly mask of stillness.

"Ah, wretch!" she cried, trembling with fear. "What have you done?"

The farmer's voice cut through the air like a knife, cold and unforgiving. "You spoke first," he said, his eyes glinting with a hint of triumph. "Now begone, woman, and shut the door."
