In the village of Azul, where the sun dipped into the horizon like a ripe mango, a tailor and his wife lived in a small hut. The husband, Kofi, was known for his skillful hands that stitched together beautiful kente cloth. His wife, Ama, was no less talented, with fingers that moved swiftly as she sewed and mended their family's clothes. She was also the one who kept their home spotless, just like a bird builds its nest.

One day, Kofi returned from his market trip to find their kitchen in disarray. Pots lay shattered on the floor, glasses were broken, and plates were cracked. He asked Ama, "What happened here? Who broke these things?" Ama replied, "How do I know?" Kofi's patience wore thin as he pressed her for an answer. "Don't play dumb with me," he said. "Who broke them?"

Ama's response was like a stubborn baobab tree refusing to bend in the wind: "I did it with the scissors." Kofi's anger grew, and he decided to test her story. He tied a rope around her waist and began to lower her into their well, saying, "Come down, Ama. Let me see if you're telling the truth."

As she descended, Ama refused to back down, repeating like a mantra: "I did it with the scissors." Kofi lowered her further, until her feet touched the water's edge. Still, she insisted that she had broken the pots and dishes with the scissors.

Kofi's rage boiled over like a pot left unattended on the fire. He let out a mighty shout and said, "Take care! You'll soon be under the water!" With one final heave, he released the rope, and Ama plunged into the well. The splash was like thunder in the still evening air.

Kofi's cry echoed through the village: "Are you satisfied now? Will you stop saying it was the scissors?" But Ama, submerged beneath the waves, signaled with her fingers as if cutting with the scissors. Kofi's heart sank like a stone, and he knew he had to act quickly. He pulled her out of the well, but still she refused to confess.

The villagers gathered around, whispering among themselves: "Ama is stubborn as a mule." And Kofi, defeated, said, "I'll let her be for now. Maybe when she's hungry and thirsty, she'll tell me the truth."

As the sun dipped below the horizon, casting the village in a warm orange glow, Ama looked up at Kofi with eyes that seemed to say: "You may have won this battle, but I'll never give in."
