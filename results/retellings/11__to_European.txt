In a far-off kingdom, there lived a Sultan whose daughter was as lovely as a rose in bloom. She had been taught the sacred words of the Koran by a wise priest, who had grown fond of her. But alas, his affection soon turned to lust, and he began to covet the girl's virtue.

The Sultan, unaware of the priest's wicked intentions, set out on a long pilgrimage, entrusting his daughter to the priest's care. The girl, however, was not one to be easily deceived. She knew the priest's true nature and refused his advances.

One day, she said to him, "Come back tomorrow, and I shall grant you an audience." But little did the priest know that the girl had a plan. She removed the ladder from the house, rendering it impossible for the priest to ascend to her chambers.

The next day, the priest arrived at the house, only to find himself unable to reach the girl's quarters. He sent a letter to the Sultan, falsely accusing his daughter of being a wanton and immoral woman. The Sultan, believing the priest's words, was consumed by anger and grief.

Upon his return from the pilgrimage, he summoned his daughter and handed her over to a group of ruthless slaves. "Cut off that girl's throat," he commanded them. But fate had other plans for the poor child.

The slaves took the girl to a secluded wooded place and began to dig her grave. As they worked, she managed to crawl away, hiding herself among the trees. When the slaves finished digging, they looked around but could not find her anywhere.

To deceive the Sultan, they slew a gazelle and poured its blood into a bottle, claiming it was the girl's lifeblood. They brought the bottle back to the palace, hoping to avoid punishment.

But fate had other plans for the priest as well. A caravan passed by the wooded place, and one of the travelers spotted the girl sitting under a tree. He took her in and brought her to his home, where she lived for some time.

One day, the Sultan's son chanced upon the girl and was struck by her beauty. He asked the man whose house she dwelt in if he could marry her, despite her supposed disability. The man agreed, and the young prince married the girl.

She bore him two children, but before long, her husband set out on a pilgrimage, leaving her behind with a ram to care for their home. While he was away, the girl had a wondrous dream in which she saw two birds perched upon her legs, and they began to grow back.

In the morning, she awoke to find that her legs had indeed been restored. Taking her children, the ram, and the two birds with her, she set out on the pilgrimage, determined to complete it.

As she journeyed, she came across a grand building at the halfway point. There, she encountered her father, brother, priest, and husband, none of whom recognized her. She told them stories of her adventures and related all that had befallen her.

The Sultan, hearing his daughter's tale, was filled with remorse for his past actions. He ordered the priest to be punished, cutting off his throat as a just reward for his wicked deeds. The girl and her family continued their pilgrimage together, finally finding peace and reconciliation.

Thus, the girl's bravery and cunning had saved her from certain death, and she lived happily ever after with her loving husband and children.
