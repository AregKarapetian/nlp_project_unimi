In a far-off land, where the sun dipped into the horizon and painted the sky with hues of crimson and gold, there lived a Sultan who was as just as he was fair. He had a daughter, a sweet and gentle soul, who was taught the sacred words of the Koran by a learned priest. But alas, the priest's heart was not pure, for he coveted the girl's beauty and wished to claim her for his own.

The Sultan, unaware of his servant's wicked intentions, entrusted his daughter to the priest while he embarked on a pilgrimage to the holy city. The priest, taking advantage of the Sultan's absence, began to plot against the girl. He would often ascend to her chambers by way of a secret ladder, but she, wise beyond her years, refused his advances.

One day, the girl said to the priest, "Come to me tomorrow," and when he arrived, she had removed the ladder from its place. The wily priest, determined to deceive the Sultan, wrote a letter to him, claiming that his daughter had fallen prey to the whispers of Satan and was now a shameless harlot.

The Sultan, gullible and trusting, returned from his pilgrimage, his heart heavy with sorrow. He summoned the girl and, upon hearing the priest's words, condemned her to death. The slaves, tasked with carrying out the sentence, took the girl to a secluded glade deep in the forest, where they dug a grave and prepared to slay her.

But fate, it seemed, had other plans for the innocent girl. As she lay helpless on the ground, she crawled away from the grave and hid among the trees. The slaves, searching high and low, could not find her, and so they slew a gazelle instead, pouring its blood into a bottle as proof of their deed.

Days passed, and the Sultan's anger cooled, but his trust in the priest remained unshaken. It was only when a caravan chanced upon the girl, sitting beneath a tree with no legs to speak of, that her fate began to change. A kind-hearted traveler took pity on her and brought her to a nearby town.

There, she lived in a humble house, her beauty and kindness winning over all who knew her. The Sultan's son, a young man of great charm and compassion, saw the girl's face and was smitten. He begged his father for permission to marry her, but when the truth about her legs came to light, he refused to abandon her.

The young couple married in secret, and soon she bore two children. But as she grew with child, a strange dream visited her: two birds perched on her legs, which had miraculously grown strong once more. When morning broke, the girl beheld her new limbs, and with them, a newfound sense of freedom.

She took her children, the ram, and the two birds, and set out on the pilgrimage to Mecca, her husband by her side. At a halfway point, they encountered a group of travelers, including the Sultan, his son, and the priest who had wronged them all.

The girl's father, recognizing his daughter at last, was overcome with grief and shame. He listened as she recounted the tale of her suffering, and when the priest tried to flee, the Sultan stayed him, saying, "Sit down until the story is finished." And so he did, until justice was served, and the priest met his just reward.

The girl and her family continued their journey together, their bond strengthened by shared trials and tribulations. In the end, they reached the holy city, where they made offerings to the Almighty and gave thanks for their deliverance.
