In days of yore, when the sun was high in the sky, a youth named Hlakanyana chanced upon a young lad tending to a flock of goats. The boy was armed with a sturdy digging-stick, and Hlakanyana proposed that they should jointly pursue after birds, for it was a grand pastime. The boy agreed to this suggestion, and together they chased the feathered creatures throughout the day.

As the sun began to set, casting its golden rays upon the landscape, Hlakanyana declared, "The time has come to roast our quarry." They stood on the banks of a winding river, where the air was filled with the sweet songs of birds. "Let us see who shall emerge from beneath the water's surface last," said Hlakanyana, his eyes glinting with mischief.

The boy agreed to this proposal, and they both plunged into the depths of the river. But when they resurfaced, it became apparent that Hlakanyana had cheated, for he emerged from the water long after the boy. "Let us try again," said the cunning fellow, his voice dripping with deceit. The boy, trusting in Hlakanyana's words, agreed to this second attempt.

Once more, they dove beneath the surface of the river, but this time Hlakanyana emerged quickly and devoured all the birds, leaving naught but their heads behind. "You have eaten them," said Hlakanyana, his voice dripping with malice, "for you came forth from the water first." The boy protested his innocence, but Hlakanyana would not be swayed.

"You must pay for my birds with that digging-stick of yours," he declared, his eyes blazing with triumph. The boy, realizing he had been outwitted, handed over the digging-stick, and Hlakanyana continued on his journey.

As he walked, he chanced upon some artisans busily crafting pots from clay. "Why do you not ask me to lend you this digging-stick?" he said with a sly smile. "It would save you the trouble of digging with your own hands." The artisans, eager for an easier life, begged Hlakanyana to lend them the stick.

But alas! As soon as they attempted to use it, the digging-stick broke into pieces. "You have broken my trust," said Hlakanyana, his voice heavy with sorrow. "This stick was given to me by a companion who ate my birds and left me naught but their heads." The artisans, filled with remorse, offered him a pot in reparation.

Hlakanyana carried the pot for some time until he came upon some young boys tending to a flock of goats. "You foolish lads," he said with a chuckle, "you only suck the milk from your charges without using any vessel. Why do you not ask me to lend you this pot?" The boys, eager to prove their worth, begged Hlakanyana to lend them the pot.

But as they attempted to use it, the pot shattered into pieces. "You have broken my trust," said Hlakanyana once more, his voice heavy with disappointment. "This pot was given to me by those who crafted it, and they had received it from the artisans who broke my digging-stick." The boys, filled with regret, offered him a goat in reparation.

Hlakanyana continued on his journey, eventually coming upon some keepers of calves. "You only suck the milk from your charges without allowing them to nurse first," he said with a sly smile. "Why do you not ask me to lend you this goat?" The keepers, eager for an easier life, begged Hlakanyana to lend them the goat.

But alas! As soon as they took possession of it, the goat perished. "You have killed my trust," said Hlakanyana, his voice heavy with sorrow. "This goat was given to me by those who tended goats, and they had received it from the boys who broke my pot." The keepers, filled with remorse, offered him a calf in reparation.

Hlakanyana carried the calf for some time until he came upon some keepers of cows. "You only suck the milk from your charges without allowing their calves to nurse first," he said with a sly smile. "Why do you not ask me to lend you this calf?" The keepers, eager for an easier life, begged Hlakanyana to lend them the calf.

But alas! As soon as they took possession of it, the calf perished. "You have killed my trust," said Hlakanyana once more, his voice heavy with disappointment. "This calf was given to me by those who kept calves, and they had received it from the keepers who killed my goat." The keepers, filled with regret, offered him a cow in reparation.

And so Hlakanyana continued on his journey, his heart heavy with sorrow, but his spirit unbroken. For he knew that one day, he would find the companion who had eaten his birds and left him naught but their heads.
