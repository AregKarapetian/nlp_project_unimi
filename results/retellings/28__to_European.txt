In days of yore, when the sun shone bright and the air was crisp, a cunning fellow named Hlakanyana roamed the land, seeking adventure and mischief. One day, as he wandered through the countryside, he chanced upon a young lad tending to his flock of goats. The boy, with a digging-stick in hand, seemed oblivious to the world around him.

Hlakanyana, ever the trickster, approached the boy and proposed a game: "Let us chase after birds together!" The boy, naive and trusting, agreed to join Hlakanyana on this wild goose chase. They pursued the feathered creatures for hours, their laughter echoing through the valleys and hills.

As the sun began to set, casting a golden glow over the landscape, Hlakanyana declared, "Now is the time to roast our quarry!" The pair arrived at the banks of a winding river, where they had been chasing their prey. Hlakanyana suggested, with a sly grin, "Let us see who can dive under the water and emerge last." The boy, eager to prove himself, agreed to this absurd challenge.

They plunged into the river, Hlakanyana emerging first, as if by magic. "Ah, I have won!" he exclaimed, feigning surprise. But when they returned to the surface, it became clear that Hlakanyana had eaten all the birds, leaving only their heads behind. The boy protested, but Hlakanyana insisted, "You must pay for my birds with your digging-stick." The boy reluctantly handed over his prized possession.

Hlakanyana continued on his journey, arriving at a village where potters were busy shaping clay into vessels. He proposed to the artisans, "Why not lend me your hands and let me use this digging-stick to dig for you?" They agreed, but alas! The first time they used it, the stick broke in two. Hlakanyana claimed, "You have broken my digging-stick, which I received from my companion, who ate my birds and left me with nothing." The potters offered him a pot as compensation.

Hlakanyana carried this new prize to a nearby village, where he encountered some young boys herding goats. He teased them, saying, "You foolish lads, only sucking the milk from your charges without using a vessel! Why not lend me this pot?" The boys agreed, and while they were milking, the pot shattered into pieces. Hlakanyana declared, "You have broken my pot, which I received from those who broke my digging-stick, which was given to them by my companion..."

The pattern continued as Hlakanyana traveled on, each time claiming a new prize only to see it destroyed in his hands. He came to the keepers of calves and proposed that they lend him their goat, which he would allow them to suckle. But when the calf died under their care, Hlakanyana claimed, "You have killed my goat, which I received from those who broke my pot..." And so it went on.

Finally, Hlakanyana arrived at a village where cowkeepers tended to their herd. He proposed that they lend him their calf, which would induce the cows to give milk freely. The keepers agreed, but alas! While in their care, the calf perished. Hlakanyana exclaimed, "You have killed my calf, which I received from those who killed my goat..." And so, with each new prize, he claimed a new victim, until finally, the cowkeepers offered him a majestic cow as compensation.

Thus, Hlakanyana continued on his journey, leaving a trail of broken promises and destroyed prizes in his wake.
