In days of yore, when Brahmadatta ruled over Benares with wisdom and justice, a young lion, born from the earth itself, roamed the forests as a mighty beast. His name was whispered in awe by the creatures of the wild.

Near the Western Ocean, where the palm trees swayed gently in the breeze and their leaves rustled softly against the vilva trees, a hare made his home. One day, after feasting on the lush grasses, he lay down beneath the shade of a young palm sapling at the foot of a venerable vilva tree. As he closed his eyes, a thought struck him: "What would become of me if this earth were to come to an end?" The very moment he pondered this question, a ripe vilva fruit fell from above and landed with a loud thud on a palm leaf.

The hare's heart skipped a beat as he leapt to his feet, convinced that the earth was indeed collapsing. Without looking back, he fled in terror, racing across the land with all haste. Another hare, who had been watching from afar, saw his friend running wildly and asked what caused such panic. "Don't ask me!" the first hare cried, but the other pressed him for an answer.

"Pray, tell me," the second hare implored, and followed close behind as they both fled in terror. More hares joined the chase, until a hundred thousand of them were racing across the land together. They caught the attention of a deer, a boar, an elk, a buffalo, a wild ox, a rhinoceros, a tiger, and an elephant.

As these noble creatures asked what caused such commotion, they too learned that the earth was coming to an end. In their fear, they joined the throng of hares in flight. The procession stretched for leagues, a mighty host of animals fleeing from some unknown calamity.

The Bodhisatta, wise and compassionate, beheld this spectacle with sorrow in his heart. He realized that the earth was not crumbling beneath their feet, but rather, it was a misinterpreted sound that had set them all on the run. With a fierce determination, he strode forward to confront the fleeing beasts.

As they halted at his roar, the Bodhisatta asked why they fled with such haste. "The earth is collapsing!" they cried in unison. He pressed them for more information: "Who saw it crumbling?" The elephants claimed knowledge of this calamity, but when questioned further, they confessed ignorance. This chain of inquiry led to the lions, who denied any knowledge; then to the tigers, who pointed to the rhinoceroses; and so on, until the deer finally admitted that the hares were the ones who had first reported the earth's collapse.

The Bodhisatta turned his attention to the hare who had started this chain of events. "Is it true," he asked gently, "that you saw the earth crumbling?" The hare nodded solemnly and described how he had been lying beneath a palm tree when the sound occurred. But the Bodhisatta was not convinced.

"I will find out the truth about this matter," he declared. Placing the hare on his back, he set off with all haste towards the grove of palms where the alleged calamity had taken place. When they arrived, the lion carefully examined the spot beneath the vilva tree and saw that a ripe fruit had indeed fallen from above.

The Bodhisatta realized that the sound was merely the thud of the falling fruit on the palm leaf, and not a sign of the earth's collapse. He gently scolded the hare for jumping to conclusions and causing such chaos among the animals. With the truth revealed, the lion reassured the herd and let them return to their homes.

Thus, thanks to the Bodhisatta's wisdom and compassion, the beasts were saved from certain destruction. If it had not been for his intervention, they would have rushed into the sea in terror, never to return. But as it was, the young lion's bravery and understanding brought peace back to the land, and all creatures rejoiced at their narrow escape.
