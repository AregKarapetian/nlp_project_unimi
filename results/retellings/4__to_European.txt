In days of yore, when King Brahmadatta held sway over Benares, a most wondrous tale unfolded. The Bodhisatta, destined to become the Buddha, took on the form of a majestic young lion and dwelled in a forest realm.

Near the Western Ocean, where palm trees swayed gently in the breeze, stood a grove of vilva trees. A humble hare resided beneath a young palm sapling, its trunk entwined with the branches of a vilva tree. One day, after sating his hunger, the hare lay down to rest beneath the palm's shade.

As he dozed, a thought crept into his mind: "If this earth were to crumble and fall, where would I flee?" His reverie was shattered by the sound of a ripe vilva fruit dropping onto a palm leaf. The sudden noise sent him leaping to his feet, convinced that the very foundations of the world were shattering.

He fled without looking back, as if death itself pursued him. Another hare chanced upon his frantic departure and asked the cause of his terror. "Pray, good sir, do not inquire," the first hare replied, but the second hare pressed on, demanding to know what ailed him.

The first hare relented, saying only that the earth was breaking apart. The second hare's curiosity now piqued, he joined in pursuit, and soon, one hundred thousand hares were fleeing together. Their mad dash caught the attention of a deer, a boar, an elk, a buffalo, a wild ox, a rhinoceros, a tiger, a lion, and an elephant.

As they asked what drove these creatures to such panic, they learned that the earth was coming to an end. And so, the entire host took flight, their numbers stretching as far as a league's length. The Bodhisatta, witnessing this chaotic exodus, realized that some misapprehension must have sparked it.

He thought, "The earth is not crumbling; surely they have mistaken some sound for its collapse." Without hesitation, he resolved to intervene and save the lives of these fleeing beasts. With the speed of a lion, he positioned himself before them at the foot of a mountain and let out three mighty roars.

Terrified by the lion's thunderous voice, the animals halted their flight and huddled together in fear. The Bodhisatta approached them, his majestic form commanding respect, and asked why they fled in such terror. "The earth is breaking apart," they replied.

"Who among you has witnessed this calamity?" he inquired. They pointed to the elephants, but those great beasts denied any knowledge of the earth's collapse. The lions were next questioned, but they too knew nothing of the supposed disaster. Thus, the inquiry continued, with each group deflecting responsibility onto another: the tigers, the rhinoceroses, the wild oxen, the buffaloes, the elks, and finally, the deer.

The deer confessed that they did not know, but the hares claimed to have been informed by one of their own. The Bodhisatta asked the accused hare to reveal what he had seen. "Yes, my lord," the hare replied, "I was lying beneath a palm sapling at the foot of a vilva tree when I heard the earth shattering. I fled in terror."

The lion's ears perked up with understanding; he knew that a ripe vilva fruit must have fallen onto a palm leaf, producing a loud thud. He thought to himself, "This poor hare has misinterpreted the sound and fled in fear." The Bodhisatta reassured the assembled animals, saying, "I will take this hare and investigate the matter further."

He placed the hare on his back and set off with the speed of a lion towards the palm grove. Upon arriving, he gently asked the hare to show him the spot where he had been lying. The hare, still trembling with fear, pointed afar and cried out, "Yonder, my lord, lies the place of dreadful sound."

The Bodhisatta approached the vilva tree and discovered the ripe fruit that had fallen onto the palm leaf. He realized that no calamity had befallen the earth. With a gentle smile, he placed the hare on his back once more and returned to the gathered animals.

As they beheld their savior's return, the Bodhisatta recounted the tale of the misinterpreted sound and the hare's misguided fear. "Do not be afraid," he reassured them. "The earth remains steadfast." And with that, he released the host of animals from their terror, allowing them to roam free once more.

Thus, it is said that if the Bodhisatta had not intervened at that time, all the beasts would have rushed into the sea and perished. But thanks to his wisdom and compassion, they escaped death's grasp.
