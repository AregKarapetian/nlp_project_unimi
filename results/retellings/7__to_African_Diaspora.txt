In the village of Akwaba, where the sun dipped into the horizon like a ripe mango, an elderly man named Kofi returned home from gathering firewood under the darkening sky. The air was heavy with the scent of rain, and the wind whispered secrets to the trees. As he walked, the first droplets began to fall, drumming against his skin like a thousand tiny fingers.

Kofi sought refuge in a hollow tree, its gnarled branches twisted like a wise elder's hands. He waited for the storm to pass, listening to the thunder rumble and the lightning flash across the sky. When the tempest had spent itself, Kofi emerged from his sanctuary, blinking away the raindrops.

As he stood there, he heard the sound of laughter and music carried on the wind. Curiosity got the better of him, and he peered into the darkness to see a group of demons dancing and singing around a fire. Their movements were unlike anything Kofi had ever seen – wild and untamed, like a herd of antelopes stampeding across the savannah.

Kofi's eyes grew wide with wonder, and he couldn't help but laugh at the absurdity of it all. The demons' leader, a tall figure with skin as black as coal, caught Kofi's eye and beckoned him to join in. But just then, one of the demons stumbled and fell, causing the others to burst into laughter.

The leader said, "Enough! Is there no one who can dance better than this fellow?" And that was when Kofi felt a spark ignite within him – a spark that had lain dormant since his youth, when he was known for his agility and skill on the dance floor. He offered to show them what he could do.

The demons cheered as Kofi danced with a ferocity that sent shivers down their spines. They clapped and whistled, urging him to perform more. The leader, impressed by Kofi's prowess, invited him to return the next night and dance again.

But there was a catch – the demons demanded a pledge from Kofi in exchange for his newfound fame. They claimed that the wen on his right cheek would make an excellent token of their friendship. And so, with a flick of his fingers, the leader removed the wen without causing Kofi any pain.

As suddenly as they had appeared, the demons vanished into thin air, leaving Kofi to ponder the miracle that had just occurred. He felt his cheek, still marveling at its smoothness after all these years.

The next day, Kofi's wife was overjoyed by her husband's newfound confidence and the strange tale he told of the demons' dance party. But their neighbor, a wicked old man named Kwame, listened with a mixture of envy and malice. He had been afflicted with a wen on his left cheek for many years, despite trying every remedy under the sun.

Kwame begged Kofi to take him to the hollow tree, promising that he would dance like a pro and win the demons' favor. But as soon as he arrived at the tree, Kwame's true intentions were revealed – he had no intention of dancing, only of deceiving the demons and claiming their reward for himself.

The demons, however, saw through Kwame's ruse. They laughed at his clumsy attempts to dance, and when they returned the wen that Kofi had left with them, it was firmly fixed on Kwame's right cheek. The wicked old man slunk away, his face now marred by two wens – a fitting punishment for his deceit.

As the villagers whispered about the tale of the demons' dance party, they would say: "A pledge is not just a token, but also a test of one's character." And Kofi and his wife would smile knowingly, their hearts filled with gratitude for the strange and wondrous events that had brought them joy.
