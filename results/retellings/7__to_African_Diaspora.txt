In a village nestled between two great rivers, where the sun dipped into the horizon like a ripe mango, an elderly man returned home from gathering firewood under the weight of a fierce thunderstorm. Seeking refuge, he stumbled upon a hollow tree, its branches twisted and gnarled like the hands of time itself. As the storm subsided, the old man cautiously emerged to find a group of demons dancing and singing with reckless abandon.

Their movements were as wild as a gazelle in flight, and the old man's curiosity got the better of him. He watched, mesmerized, as one demon danced alone, his clumsiness drawing snickers from the others. The leader of the demons, a towering figure with eyes that shone like stars on a clear night, growled, "Enough! Is there no one who can dance better than this fool?"

The old man's heart stirred within him, recalling the days when he was young and agile, his feet moving in perfect harmony to the rhythm of life. He offered to show off his skills, and with a flourish, began to dance before the demons. They cheered and clapped, their faces aglow with delight.

"Ah, you are a true master!" exclaimed the leader, offering him a cup of sak, a brew that flowed like honey from the gods. "We beg of you, dance for us again!"

The old man's spirit soared, his feet moving in perfect syncopation as he twirled and leaped with abandon. The demons roared with laughter, their joy infectious. When the night wore on, the leader approached him, a sly smile spreading across his face.

"You have pleased us greatly," he said. "But you must leave something behind, a token of your visit. That wen on your right cheek will make an excellent pledge."

With a deft touch, the demon removed the wen, leaving the old man's skin smooth and unblemished. As the demons vanished into thin air, the old man stumbled out of the hollow tree, his hand reaching for his cheek in wonder.

The news of his encounter with the demons spread like wildfire through the village, and soon his neighbor, a wicked and cantankerous old man, heard of it. This man had long suffered from a wen on his left cheek, which no amount of medicine or prayer could cure.

He begged his neighbor to tell him more about the hollow tree and the demons who dwelled within. The good old man shared the tale with him, cautioning that one must be true in their intentions when dealing with such beings.

The wicked old man sneered at the warning but decided to try his luck. He hid in the hollow tree just before sunset, waiting for the demons to appear. When they did, he crept out of his hiding-place, flourishing a fan and attempting to dance.

But alas! His movements were as awkward as a newborn fawn on its first steps. The demons jeered at him, their faces twisted with disgust. "You dance like a fool!" one of them cried. "Leave now, before we return the pledge you left behind!"

With that, they flung the wen back onto his right cheek, where it stuck fast, refusing to budge. The wicked old man slunk away, his face now marred by two wens, a fitting punishment for his deceitful heart.

As the sun dipped below the horizon, the villagers whispered among themselves about the encounter between the demons and the wicked old man. They shook their heads in dismay, saying, "A lie will not be tolerated by the spirits; only truth and sincerity can bring reward."
