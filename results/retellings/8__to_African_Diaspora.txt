In a village nestled in the heart of the mountains, where the air was sweet with the scent of blooming flowers, there lived a man who had grown weary from his journey. As night began to fall, he sought refuge in the hollow trunk of an ancient tree, its gnarled branches twisted like withered fingers.

But little did he know, the tree was home to a group of mischievous spirits known as the Yoruba Orishas. They descended upon the tree, their laughter and music echoing through the night air like the gentle lapping of waves on a summer shore. The man, initially frightened by their sudden appearance, soon found himself entranced by their revelry.

As the night wore on, the Orishas invited him to join in their merriment, and he, unable to resist their infectious joy, crept out of his hiding place to dance with them under the starry sky. When dawn began to break, they bid him farewell, but not before taking a token from his forehead – a wen that had plagued him for years.

The man returned home, his heart full of cheer and his face free from the wen's burden. He shared his tale with his friends and family, who rejoiced at his good fortune. But one neighbor, consumed by envy, listened to his story with growing unease.

This envious neighbor had long suffered from a wen himself, and as he heard of his friend's luck, a pang of jealousy struck him like a hot knife through butter. He set out to find the ancient tree, determined to claim its magic for himself. When he finally stumbled upon it, the Orishas welcomed him with open arms, thinking him to be their former companion.

With a mischievous grin, one of the Orishas pulled out the pawned wen and stuck it onto the man's forehead, right on top of his existing wen. The envious neighbor returned home, tears streaming down his face as he realized that his covetous heart had led him astray.

As the villagers gathered to hear the tale, they nodded in agreement with the wise words of their elder: "Aje is a jealous spirit; she takes from those who cannot see the good fortune of others." And so, the lesson was passed down through generations, reminding all that true happiness comes not from coveting what others have, but from embracing one's own blessings.
